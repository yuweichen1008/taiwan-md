# Morris Chang

Morris Chang, the legendary entrepreneur hailed as the "Godfather of Semiconductors," is the founder of Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC). He not only created the world's first dedicated semiconductor foundry but also pioneered a business model that fundamentally reshaped the global technology industry. From his rise as a Chinese-American executive at Texas Instruments to his return to Taiwan to establish TSMC, his life trajectory mirrors the evolution of the global semiconductor industry and cemented Taiwan's critical position in the worldwide tech supply chain.

## 30-Second Overview

**Why should the world know Morris Chang?**

TSMC, the company Morris Chang founded, is not only the world's largest chip foundry but also a cornerstone of modern digital civilization. From smartphones and computers to artificial intelligence chips, the vast majority of the world's advanced semiconductors are manufactured by TSMC. The "pure-play foundry" business model he pioneered allowed countless tech companies to focus on chip design without investing billions in building fabrication plants, fundamentally transforming the global technology ecosystem.

TSMC is often called Taiwan's "Silicon Shield," carrying significant strategic importance in geopolitics. Morris Chang is not merely a successful entrepreneur but a world-changing innovator whose story is a legend of vision, perseverance, and business wisdom.

## Early Life and Education

### Growing Up in Turbulent Times

**Birth and family background:**
On July 10, 1931, Morris Chang was born in Ningbo, Zhejiang Province, China. His father, Zhang Weiguan, was a banker, and his mother, Xu Yunzheng, came from a scholarly family. In those turbulent times, his family's emphasis on education laid a strong cultural foundation for his future.

**A childhood of constant migration:**
Due to war, Morris Chang's childhood was marked by constant relocation. From Ningbo to Shanghai, Nanjing, Chongqing, back to Shanghai, then to Guangzhou, Hong Kong, and finally the United States -- this itinerant upbringing cultivated his adaptability and international perspective.

**Schooling in Hong Kong:**
In 1945, the 14-year-old Morris Chang moved with his family to Hong Kong, where he received his secondary education under the British colonial system. Hong Kong's cosmopolitan environment and English-language education laid an important foundation for his later studies and career in the United States.

### The Pivotal Turn to America

**A brief stint at Harvard:**
In 1949, the 18-year-old Morris Chang enrolled at Harvard University, initially studying literature. However, after a year, lacking passion for the subject and facing financial considerations, he transferred to the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT).

**Engineering education at MIT:**
At MIT, Morris Chang chose mechanical engineering as his major. This seemingly incidental decision actually laid the groundwork for his later success in manufacturing. MIT's rigorous engineering program cultivated his logical thinking and problem-solving abilities.

**Graduating in 1952:**
Morris Chang earned his bachelor's degree in mechanical engineering from MIT in 1952. Graduating during the Korean War, his status as a foreign student prevented him from obtaining U.S. military-related positions -- a restriction that ultimately steered him toward civilian industry.

## Early Career: Sylvania

### Entering the Semiconductor Industry

**The 1955 opportunity:**
After graduation, Morris Chang worked at Sylvania for three years, marking his first foray into the semiconductor industry. The semiconductor sector was still in its infancy, but Chang keenly recognized the enormous potential of this emerging field.

**Developing technical expertise:**
At Sylvania, Morris Chang was responsible for semiconductor device manufacturing, learning the fundamentals of semiconductor fabrication processes. This experience acquainted him with the complexity and precision of semiconductor manufacturing and fostered his attention to technical detail.

**Early signs of management talent:**
Even in entry-level technical positions, Morris Chang demonstrated exceptional management skills. His ability to effectively organize teams and solve technical problems laid the foundation for his future career in management.

## The Texas Instruments Era: Rising Through the Ranks

### The Critical Turn of 1958

**Joining Texas Instruments:**
In 1958, Morris Chang joined Texas Instruments (TI), the most important turning point in his career. TI was aggressively expanding its semiconductor business at the time, providing him with an ideal platform for growth.

**From engineer to executive:**
At Texas Instruments, Morris Chang started as a semiconductor engineer overseeing wafer production. He excelled not only technically but, more importantly, demonstrated outstanding management capabilities that quickly caught the attention of senior leadership.

**Contributions to process improvement:**
During his time at TI, Morris Chang significantly improved the company's semiconductor manufacturing processes, boosting product yield and production efficiency. These technical improvements saved the company substantial costs and earned him a strong reputation.

### Advanced Studies at Stanford

**The 1961 opportunity:**
In 1961, Texas Instruments sponsored Morris Chang to pursue a doctoral degree in electrical engineering at Stanford University -- a testament to the company's recognition of his abilities and the American corporate commitment to talent development.

**Earning the doctorate:**
In 1964, Morris Chang earned his Ph.D. in electrical engineering from Stanford. This not only enhanced his technical expertise but, more importantly, expanded his vision and professional network.

**Returning to a new role:**
After completing his doctorate, Morris Chang returned to Texas Instruments with more senior management positions. He served successively as general manager of the germanium transistor division, silicon transistor division, and integrated circuit division, steadily advancing in his career.

### Breaking the Glass Ceiling

**The historic promotion of 1972:**
In 1972, Morris Chang was promoted to group vice president of Texas Instruments, becoming one of the highest-ranking Chinese-American executives at a major U.S. corporation. This promotion was an exceptionally rare achievement in the American business landscape of that era.

**Leading the semiconductor group:**
Morris Chang simultaneously served as general manager of TI's semiconductor group, overseeing the company's most important business division. Under his leadership, TI's semiconductor operations grew rapidly, establishing the company as a major global semiconductor supplier.

**Summing up 25 years in America:**
During his 25 years at Texas Instruments, Morris Chang rose from entry-level engineer to senior executive, accumulating extensive technical and management experience. Most importantly, he developed a profound understanding of the semiconductor industry's dynamics and business logic.

## Taiwan's Call: Leading ITRI

### The 1985 Life-Changing Decision

**The invitation to return:**
In 1985, Morris Chang was invited by ITRI chairman Xu Xianshu, Premier Yu Kuo-hwa, and Minister without Portfolio K.T. Li to serve as president of the Industrial Technology Research Institute (ITRI). This decision altered the course of his life and the destiny of Taiwan's technology industry.

**The courage to leave his comfort zone:**
At 54, Morris Chang already had a successful career and comfortable life in America. Choosing to return to Taiwan was a decision full of risk and challenge, reflecting his sense of mission toward Taiwan's technological development.

**Reforming ITRI:**
During his tenure as ITRI president, Morris Chang vigorously promoted the integration of research and industry, elevating Taiwan's technological innovation capacity. His management philosophy and international perspective brought a new direction to the institute.

### Assessing Taiwan's Semiconductor Potential

**Analyzing the industrial landscape:**
Morris Chang conducted a thorough analysis of Taiwan's industrial environment and advantages. He concluded that Taiwan possessed excellent engineering talent, lower costs, and flexible manufacturing capabilities -- conditions well suited for developing semiconductor manufacturing.

**Conceiving the foundry model:**
During his time at ITRI, Morris Chang began formulating a revolutionary business concept: the dedicated semiconductor foundry. This vision emerged from his deep insight into industry trends and was the crystallization of decades of industry experience.

**The importance of government support:**
Morris Chang recognized that developing a semiconductor industry required strong government support, including capital investment, policy backing, and talent cultivation. He actively communicated with government officials to prepare for TSMC's establishment.

## The Birth of TSMC: Innovation in 1987

### The Foundry Model Revolution

**A breakthrough concept:**
In 1987, Morris Chang introduced the innovative concept of the "dedicated semiconductor foundry." Unlike the traditional Integrated Device Manufacturer (IDM) model, TSMC would focus exclusively on manufacturing chips for clients rather than designing its own products.

**A revolutionary business model:**
The revolutionary nature of this model lay in enabling companies without the resources to build fabrication plants to design advanced chips, significantly lowering the barrier to entry in the semiconductor industry and fostering an explosion of innovation.

**Impact on the industry ecosystem:**
The foundry model created an entirely new industry ecosystem, allowing design companies to focus on innovation while foundries focused on manufacturing. This specialization and division of labor elevated the efficiency of the entire industry.

### The Founding of TSMC

**The historic moment of February 21, 1987:**
On February 21, 1987, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company was officially established with an initial capital of NT$22 billion. It was the result of collaboration among the government, private enterprises, and foreign investors.

**Equity structure design:**
TSMC's equity structure included ITRI, Dutch company Philips, and Taiwanese private enterprises. This diversified ownership brought technology, capital, and market resources to the company.

**Morris Chang's dual role:**
Morris Chang served as both chairman and president (later CEO) of TSMC, responsible for overall strategy and daily operations. His leadership style blended the efficiency of American management with the wisdom of Chinese culture.

## TSMC's Growth Journey

### Early Challenges and Breakthroughs

**1987-1990: The difficult early years:**
TSMC faced enormous challenges in its early days. The dedicated foundry was an entirely new business model that the market viewed with skepticism. Clients needed time to accept the concept, and Morris Chang had to simultaneously build manufacturing capabilities and convince potential customers.

**Building technical capabilities:**
TSMC's initial technology came from transfers from ITRI and collaboration with Philips. Morris Chang led his team in rapidly learning and improving process technology, establishing reliable manufacturing capabilities.

**Winning the first clients:**
TSMC's earliest clients were primarily American fabless semiconductor companies -- firms that needed dedicated foundry services, providing TSMC with its early business foundation.

### Rapid Growth in the 1990s

**Technology leadership strategy:**
In the 1990s, Morris Chang formulated a "technology leadership" strategy, investing heavily in R&D to ensure TSMC maintained its edge in process technology. This strategy laid the foundation for the company's long-term development.

**Capacity expansion and global presence:**
As orders increased, TSMC aggressively expanded capacity, constructing multiple fabs in Taiwan while also considering global expansion to be closer to key clients and markets.

**Client diversification:**
TSMC's client base expanded continuously, growing from early American fabless companies to include the world's leading semiconductor design firms, such as Qualcomm, Broadcom, and NVIDIA.

### Leadership in the 21st Century

**The advanced process race:**
Entering the 21st century, semiconductor process technology became increasingly difficult and expensive to develop. Through sustained R&D investment and technological innovation, TSMC maintained its leadership in advanced processes.

**From 28nm to 5nm and beyond:**
From 28nm to 16nm, 7nm, then 5nm and 3nm, TSMC maintained its technological lead at each generation of advanced processes, solidifying its dominance in the high-end market.

**The Apple partnership milestone:**
The partnership with Apple, particularly manufacturing processors for iPhone and iPad, became a defining milestone in TSMC's history, significantly boosting the company's revenue and market position.

## Business Philosophy and Management Principles

### Commitment to Technological Innovation

**Prioritizing R&D investment:**
Morris Chang consistently emphasized the importance of technological innovation. TSMC allocates a significant portion of annual revenue to R&D, ensuring its technological edge. This commitment to long-term investment is a key factor in the company's success.

**Pursuing Moore's Law:**
In an era when Moore's Law faces increasing challenges, Morris Chang led TSMC in continuously advancing process technology, extending the vitality of Moore's Law and making significant contributions to the semiconductor industry's progress.

**Balancing technology and market demands:**
Morris Chang excelled at finding the equilibrium between technological leadership and market demand -- maintaining technical advantages while ensuring their commercial viability and avoiding excessive costs from overly aggressive advancement.

### Talent Development and Corporate Culture

**Investing in talent:**
Morris Chang placed great importance on nurturing and developing talent, establishing comprehensive training programs. He believed that people are a company's most important asset and that only by cultivating outstanding talent can an enterprise achieve sustained growth.

**Internationalized management style:**
Morris Chang brought the efficiency and transparency of American management practices to TSMC, establishing a modern corporate governance structure while incorporating the wisdom of Chinese culture to create a distinctive organizational ethos.

## Retirement and Succession

### The First Retirement in 2005

**Initiating the succession plan:**
In 2005, the 74-year-old Morris Chang announced his retirement, handing the CEO role to Rick Tsai. This was part of a carefully prepared succession plan, reflecting his commitment to the enterprise's long-term sustainability.

**Retaining the chairmanship:**
Although stepping down as CEO, Morris Chang retained the chairmanship, continuing to participate in major strategic decisions and providing guidance and support to the new management team.

**Life after retirement:**
Morris Chang did not completely withdraw from business activities after retirement. He actively participated in various forums and conferences, sharing his experience and insights to contribute to industry development.

### The 2009 Comeback

**The financial crisis challenge:**
The 2008 global financial crisis had a significant impact on TSMC, as the company faced declining performance and intensifying competition. At this critical juncture, Morris Chang decided to return.

**Resuming the CEO role:**
In 2009, the 78-year-old Morris Chang reassumed the role of TSMC CEO, personally leading the company through its difficulties. His return stabilized market confidence and provided the leadership needed for recovery.

**Cultivating the next generation:**
During his second tenure, Morris Chang focused on grooming successors, including Mark Liu and C.C. Wei, preparing the company's talent pipeline for the future.

### The Final Retirement in 2018

**Completing the succession:**
In June 2018, the 87-year-old Morris Chang officially retired, concluding his legendary 31-year journey at TSMC. He handed the chairmanship to Mark Liu and the CEO position to C.C. Wei.

**Establishing the dual-leadership structure:**
Morris Chang established a "dual-leadership" system: Mark Liu as chairman handling external affairs and C.C. Wei as CEO managing internal operations. This arrangement was designed to ensure stable corporate development.

**A model of graceful retirement:**
Morris Chang's retirement is regarded as a textbook example of corporate succession. He stepped aside at the right moment, giving the next generation of leaders room to perform while ensuring a smooth transition.

## Impact on the Global Semiconductor Industry

### Business Model Innovation

**The spread of the foundry model:**
The dedicated foundry model created by Morris Chang has become one of the semiconductor industry's standard business models. Hundreds of fabless semiconductor companies worldwide rely on foundry services, a model that has greatly accelerated industry development.

**Reshaping the industry ecosystem:**
The foundry model reshaped the semiconductor industry's ecosystem, promoting specialization and division of labor, improving industry efficiency, and lowering the barriers to innovation so that more companies could participate in chip design.

**Building global supply chains:**
TSMC became a critical node in the global semiconductor supply chain, providing manufacturing services to tech companies around the world and establishing a truly globalized semiconductor supply network.

### Driving Technological Progress

**Leading-edge process technology:**
Under Morris Chang's leadership, TSMC maintained its global lead in process technology, driving technological progress across the semiconductor industry and extending the relevance of Moore's Law.

**Democratizing advanced processes:**
TSMC's advanced process services enabled smaller companies to access cutting-edge technology, effectively "democratizing" advanced manufacturing and fueling a surge of innovation.

**Pioneering new technology domains:**
From traditional digital chips to AI processors, from consumer electronics to automotive semiconductors, TSMC provides manufacturing support for a wide range of emerging technology domains, driving the application of technological innovation.
