# Mountains and Hiking Culture

At 5 AM on the summit of Yushan (Jade Mountain), hundreds of hikers stand in sub-zero temperatures waiting for sunrise. As the first ray of sunlight spills across the ridgeline of the Central Mountain Range, this 3,952-meter peak -- the highest in Northeast Asia -- once again bears witness to Taiwan's deep love of mountains. On an island of just 36,000 square kilometers, an astonishing 268 peaks rise above 3,000 meters, and mountainous terrain accounts for two-thirds of the total land area. From the scientific surveys of the Japanese colonial era to the postwar Baiyue (Hundred Peaks) tradition, hiking in Taiwan is far more than recreation -- it is a spiritual pursuit and a form of cultural identity.

> Taiwan has the world's highest density of tall mountains, with 268 peaks above 3,000 meters distributed across five major ranges: the Central Mountain Range, Xueshan Range, Yushan Range, Coastal Range, and Alishan Range. Of these, 100 have been designated the "Baiyue" (Taiwan's Hundred Peaks), forming a pilgrimage for mountaineers.

## Why It Matters

Taiwan's mountains are more than geographic landmarks -- they carry cultural and spiritual significance. In a rapidly urbanizing society, mountains offer space for reconnecting with nature, challenging oneself, and finding perspective. The Baiyue tradition has nurtured generation after generation of mountain enthusiasts who pursue not only the achievement of reaching summits but also learn to respect nature, practice teamwork, and transcend personal limits. However, as the hiking population surges, balancing mountain access with ecological conservation has become one of Taiwan's most pressing challenges.

## Geographic Overview of Taiwan's Mountains

### The Five Major Mountain Ranges

**Central Mountain Range:**

- Taiwan's backbone, stretching roughly 270 km from north to south
- Home to the most peaks above 3,000 meters (over 170)
- Major summits: Xiuguluan (3,805 m), Mabolasi (3,785 m), Yushan North Peak (3,858 m)

**Xueshan Range:**

- Northern Taiwan's natural barrier, containing Xueshan Main Peak (3,886 m)
- Taiwan's second-highest peak, often called the "Roof of Taiwan"
- Major summits: Dabajian (3,492 m), Pintian (3,524 m)

**Yushan Range:**

- Home to Yushan Main Peak (3,952 m), the highest in Northeast Asia
- The Yushan massif includes the Front, East, West, North, and South Peaks
- Major summits: Dongpu (3,393 m), Batongguan (3,335 m)

**Coastal Range:**

- Taiwan's only eastern seaboard mountain range
- Lower in elevation, with the highest peak being Xingang (1,682 m)
- Geologically young, with abundant volcanic landforms

**Alishan Range:**

- An important range in the southwest
- Major summits: Data (2,663 m), Xiaota (2,484 m)
- Famed for its sunrises, sea of clouds, sunsets, forests, and mountain railway

### Geology and Climate

**Geological Composition:**

- Primarily slate, metamorphic sandstone, and limestone
- Extensive faulting and frequent earthquakes
- Hard rock formations producing dramatic mountain scenery

**Climate Zones by Elevation:**

- **Below 2,500 m:** Subtropical and temperate climate
- **2,500-3,500 m:** Cool temperate climate, snowfall possible in winter
- **Above 3,500 m:** Alpine climate, cold year-round

**Notable Landscapes:**

- Cirques (Xueshan Cirque, Jiaming Lake)
- Cliff faces (Dabajian, Zhongyangjian)
- Alpine meadows (Hehuan, Qilai Main Peak)
- Primeval forests (Smangus, Lalashan)

## The Rise of the Baiyue Tradition

### How the Hundred Peaks Were Chosen

**The 1971 Baiyue List:**
Selected by mountaineering pioneers including Lin Wen-an, the criteria were:

1. Elevation above 3,000 meters
2. Majestic appearance or special significance
3. Presence of a triangulation point or forest marker
4. At least 100 meters of independent prominence between peaks

**The Five Great Mountains, Three Sharp Peaks, and One Marvel:**
The nine most iconic high mountains of Taiwan:

- **Five Great Mountains:** Yushan, Xueshan, Xiuguluan, Nanhu Dashan, Beidawu
- **Three Sharp Peaks:** Zhongyangjian, Dabajian, Dafen Jianshan
- **One Marvel:** Qilai Main Peak

### The Development of Mountaineering

**Japanese Colonial Period (1895-1945):**

- Focused on academic surveys and topographic mapping
- Japanese scholars like Mori Ushinosuke conducted alpine expeditions
- Early hiking trails and mountain huts established

**Postwar Revival (1945-1970):**

- Military restrictions limited civilian access to mountains
- University mountaineering clubs were the primary organized groups
- Key routes: Yushan, Xueshan, Hehuan

**The Baiyue Craze (1970-1990):**

- The 1971 Baiyue list sparked a peak-bagging movement
- Mountaineering clubs proliferated
- Equipment and techniques steadily matured
- Media attention increased

**The Era of Mass Participation (1990-Present):**

- Internet access made trail information widely available
- Improved gear lowered barriers to entry
- Government "open mountain" policies
- Rapid growth in hiker numbers

## Mountain Hut Systems and Hiking Routes

### Major Mountain Huts

Taiwan's high mountain areas have dozens of huts providing overnight shelter for hikers:

**Yushan Route Huts:**

- **Paiyun Lodge** (3,402 m): The staging point before the summit push, capacity 92 people
- **Baimulin Lodge**: Key waypoint for Yushan massif traverses

**Xueshan Route Huts:**

- **369 Lodge** (3,100 m): Mid-route rest stop on the Xueshan trail
- **Xueshan East Peak Shelter**: Small emergency shelter

**Nanhu Dashan Route:**

- **Yunling Lodge** (3,200 m): Beside the Nanhu cirque, with spectacular views
- **Chenggong Lodge**: Key stop along Central Mountain Range traverses

**Jiaming Lake Route:**

- **Xiangyang Lodge** (2,800 m): Trailhead for the Jiaming Lake National Trail
- **Jiaming Lake Shelter**: Basic lakeside emergency shelter

### Classic Hiking Routes

**Beginner Routes:**

- **Hehuan Main Peak** (3,417 m): Taiwan's most accessible Baiyue peak
- **Shimen** (3,237 m): Reachable in 30 minutes from the trailhead
- **Hehuan East Peak** (3,421 m): Stunning panoramic grassland ridge walk

**Intermediate Routes:**

- **Yushan Main Peak**: A pilgrimage to Northeast Asia's highest summit
- **Xueshan Main Peak**: Classic cirque terrain and the famous dark forest
- **Qilai South Peak**: Spectacular scenery in Taroko National Park

**Advanced Routes:**

- **The Holy Ridge (Shenglingxian)**: The iconic traverse from Xueshan to Dabajian
- **Southern Section Two**: The most scenic alpine traverse along the Central Mountain Range
- **Mabo Traverse**: An extreme challenge in the southern Central Mountain Range

## Jiaming Lake: Tears of the Angels

### An Alpine Lake Wonder

Jiaming Lake sits at 3,310 meters between Xiangyang Mountain and Sanchajianshan, making it Taiwan's second-highest alpine lake. This oval-shaped natural lake, approximately 120 meters in diameter and 6 to 8 meters deep, has been dubbed "Tears of the Angels" for its jewel-like azure waters.

**Geological Origins:**

- A relic of the ice age roughly 10,000 years ago
- Formed by water collecting in a cirque depression
- Fed primarily by rain and snowmelt
- Never dries up; may freeze in winter

**Ecological Features:**

- No fish survive in the lake (elevation too high)
- Surrounded by alpine meadow vegetation
- Home to rare plants such as Taiwanese quillwort
- An important stopover for migratory birds

### Hiking Demand and Conservation Challenges

**Popularity:**
Since media coverage in the 2000s, Jiaming Lake has become one of Taiwan's most sought-after hiking destinations:

- Over 50,000 permit applications per year
- Mountain hut beds are extremely hard to secure
- Weekends and holidays are booked out well in advance

**Environmental Impact:**
Heavy foot traffic brings environmental pressures:

- Overuse of hiking trails
- Waste and human waste disposal challenges
- Trampling of alpine vegetation
- Changes in lake water quality

**Management Measures:**

- Total visitor caps enforced
- Staggered application and time-slot systems
- Increased ranger patrols
- Promotion of Leave No Trace education

## Mountain Access Permits and Safety Management

### The Permit System

**Historical Context:**

- 1950s-1980s: Strict restrictions for military security reasons
- 1980s-2000s: Gradual liberalization and simplified application processes
- 2000s onward: Safety management as the primary focus

**Current Permit Categories:**

1. **Class A:** General restricted areas, online application only
2. **Class B:** Special restricted areas, advance application with review required
3. **Class C:** National security-sensitive zones, strictly controlled

**Convenient Application Options:**

- Online application system (Mountain Area Guide Information Network)
- Mobile app for real-time applications
- 24-hour customer service support
- Multilingual services

### Mountain Safety Management

**Rescue System:**

- National Fire Agency aerial rescue team
- County and city fire department mountain rescue teams
- Civilian volunteer rescue organizations
- Indigenous community search and rescue teams

**Required Safety Equipment:**

- GPS device or satellite communicator
- Emergency shelter gear (tent, sleeping bag)
- First-aid kit and basic medications
- Adequate food and water supplies

**Risk Management Measures:**

- Weather forecasts and real-time alerts
- Trail condition reporting system
- Hiking plan review process
- Insurance recommendations

## Indigenous Peoples and Mountain Culture

### Traditional Territories and Sacred Mountains

**Traditional Indigenous Mountain Names:**
Many peaks hold sacred meaning in indigenous languages:

- **Yushan:** Known to the Bunun people as "Patungkuanu" (Mountain of Quartz)
- **Xueshan:** Known to the Atayal people as "Babo Hagai" (Rocky Mountain)
- **Dabajian:** An Atayal sacred mountain called "Papak Waqa"

**Sacred Mountain Beliefs and Taboos:**

- Certain peaks are considered dwelling places of ancestral spirits and may not be climbed casually
- Prayers and blessing rituals are performed before hiking
- Mountain taboos and traditional ecological wisdom are observed

**Traditional Ecological Knowledge:**
Indigenous peoples have accumulated millennia of mountain wisdom:

- Weather observation and prediction
- Plant identification and utilization
- Animal behavior observation
- Route selection and safety judgment

### Indigenous Guides and Eco-Tourism

**Indigenous Guide Programs:**

- Drawing on indigenous mountain knowledge
- Creating employment opportunities for indigenous communities
- Passing down traditional culture
- Fostering cross-cultural exchange

**Success Stories:**

- **Smangus:** An Atayal village that developed community-based eco-tourism
- **Jialan:** Paiwan indigenous guide services
- **Luanshan:** A Bunun "forest museum"

## Ecology and Sustainable Hiking

### Alpine Ecosystems

**Vertical Vegetation Zones:**

- **1,500-2,500 m:** Mixed conifer-broadleaf forest (Formosan cypress, Taiwan cedar)
- **2,500-3,000 m:** Coniferous forest (Taiwan fir, Taiwan hemlock)
- **Above 3,000 m:** Alpine meadows and scrub

**Endemic and Rare Species:**

- **Formosan black bear:** Flagship species of high-mountain forests
- **Mikado pheasant:** A Taiwan-endemic pheasant found at mid-to-high elevations
- **Taiwan beech:** An ice age relict plant
- **Yushan thistle:** An alpine endemic plant

**Ecological Threats:**

- Climate change pushing plant distribution to higher elevations
- Invasive species (such as Bidens pilosa)
- Human disturbance and habitat fragmentation
- Environmental impact from hiking activity

### The Leave No Trace Movement

**LNT Principles:**

1. Plan ahead and prepare
2. Travel and camp on durable surfaces
3. Dispose of waste properly
4. Leave what you find
5. Minimize campfire impact
6. Respect wildlife
7. Be considerate of other visitors

**Outreach Strategies:**

- Mountaineering education campaigns
- Volunteer training and certification
- Trail maintenance work holidays
- Corporate social responsibility partnerships

### Sustainable Forest Management

**National Park Management:**

- Taroko National Park (Qilai Range)
- Shei-Pa National Park (Xueshan, Dabajian)
- Yushan National Park (Yushan massif)

**Carrying Capacity Management:**

- Ecological carrying capacity as the baseline
- Dynamic adjustment of visitor numbers
- Seasonal access restrictions
- Route diversification strategies

**Sustainable Facilities:**

- Eco-friendly mountain hut design (solar power, rainwater harvesting)
- Composting toilets
- Ecological trail engineering methods
- Waste reduction and recycling programs

## The Social Significance of Hiking Culture

### Spiritual Value

**Self-Challenge and Growth:**
Hiking offers modern people rare opportunities for personal challenge:

- Pushing physical limits
- Strengthening willpower
- Learning teamwork
- Developing risk assessment skills

**Mental Clarity and Healing:**

- Escaping urban noise, returning to nature
- Living in the present, relieving daily stress
- Finding spiritual fulfillment through scenic beauty
- Embracing simplicity, reconsidering life's priorities

### Community and Legacy

**Hiking Community Culture:**

- Deep friendships forged among fellow hikers
- Passing down mountaineering knowledge and skills
- The spirit of mutual aid on the mountain
- Generational continuity of mountain culture

**The Baiyue Completion Achievement:**
Completing all 100 Baiyue peaks is considered a major milestone:

- A testament to personal perseverance and determination
- An accumulation of alpine knowledge and skill
- A source of community identity and belonging
- A profound experience of Taiwan's mountain culture

## Future Challenges

### Climate Change Adaptation

**Changes in Alpine Environments:**

- Rising temperatures affecting ecological distribution
- Shifting snowfall patterns
- Increasing extreme weather events
- Heightened geological hazard risks

**Adaptation Strategies:**

- Strengthening weather monitoring and early warning systems
- Adjusting hiking seasons and routes
- Improving emergency response capabilities
- Expanding ecological monitoring and research

### Managing Hiker Numbers

**The Trend Toward Mass Participation:**

- Growing engagement among younger generations
- Increasing numbers of international visitors
- Social media amplifying interest
- Lower equipment and technical barriers

**Management Challenges:**

- Pressure on environmental carrying capacity
- Increasing complexity of safety management
- Balancing commercialization with conservation
- Protecting indigenous rights

### Building Smart Mountains

**Digital Technology Applications:**

- IoT-based environmental monitoring
- AI-assisted rescue and positioning
- Virtual reality mountain experiences
- Big data analysis of visitor behavior

**Sustainable Development Goals:**

- Conservation as the top priority
- Co-management with indigenous peoples
- Deepening educational outreach
- International knowledge exchange

## The Mountain as a Classroom

Taiwan's mountains are more than natural landscapes -- they are a school without walls. Here, people learn humility in the face of nature's power, perseverance when the path grows difficult, generosity through sharing with fellow hikers, and gratitude for everything the mountains provide.

From the scientific expeditions of the Japanese colonial era to today's Baiyue tradition, Taiwan's mountains have witnessed social transformation and the evolution of spiritual aspiration. In an age of globalization and urbanization, Taiwan's mountain culture offers a space to return to nature and reexamine the meaning of life. These 268 peaks above 3,000 meters are not merely geographic markers -- they are symbols of Taiwan's spiritual homeland.
