# Taiwan’s New Religious Movements and Spiritual Culture

## 30-second overview

After World War II, Taiwan experienced a powerful religious revival. The island’s spiritual landscape expanded beyond traditional folk belief into organized movements that combined ethics, education, and social service. The most emblematic are the Buddhist “Four Great Mountains” (四大山頭)—Tzu Chi (慈濟), Fo Guang Shan (佛光山), Dharma Drum Mountain (法鼓山), and Chung Tai Chan Monastery (中台禪寺)—as well as Yiguandao (一貫道), a syncretic tradition blending Confucianism, Buddhism, and Daoism. These groups did more than shape faith; they built hospitals, schools, global charity networks, and a civic ethos that deeply influenced Taiwan’s modern identity.

**Keywords:** Humanistic Buddhism (人間佛教), Four Great Mountains, Yiguandao, religious freedom, charity, spiritual culture

## Why it matters

### A moral infrastructure behind the “economic miracle”

Taiwan’s rapid modernization demanded more than factories and export policies; it required social trust, volunteerism, and shared ethics. New religious movements provided frameworks for compassion, discipline, and civic participation. Concepts like Tzu Chi’s “Great Love” (大愛) or Fo Guang Shan’s Humanistic Buddhism (人間佛教) became part of everyday language and behavior, shaping how communities responded to hardship.

### A distinct Taiwanese model of modern religion

Taiwan offers a rare case where traditional spirituality adapted to urbanization without losing its cultural roots. The island’s “Taiwan model” keeps ritual and ancestral sensibility while building professional institutions—hospitals, universities, and media outlets—that meet modern needs. This hybrid model offers insights to other East Asian and Chinese-speaking societies negotiating similar transformations.

### Soft power in action

Taiwanese religious organizations are visible on the global stage: Tzu Chi’s disaster relief teams, Fo Guang Shan’s international temples, and Dharma Drum’s meditation programs have become the island’s cultural ambassadors. Their humanitarian work communicates Taiwan’s values far beyond its borders.

## Postwar conditions: from restraint to revival

Under Japanese rule, traditional religions were tightly regulated. After 1945, the political environment remained authoritarian, but religious policy gradually loosened. In the 1950s, the government recognized religion as a stabilizing force in a Cold War context. Legal frameworks such as the Temple Administration Regulations (寺廟監督條例) and the Civil Associations Act (人民團體組織法) created paths for formal religious organization and public participation.

At the same time, rapid urbanization fractured village-based social networks. New religious communities became a substitute for lost social support—offering belonging, ethical guidance, and practical aid to people navigating modern life.

## The Buddhist “Four Great Mountains”

### Tzu Chi (慈濟): charity as spiritual practice

Founded in Hualien in 1966 by Master Cheng Yen (證嚴法師), Tzu Chi began with 30 housewives saving small daily coins in bamboo tubes. Its philosophy emphasizes compassion in action: charity is not merely generosity, but a path of cultivation. Tzu Chi’s “Four Great Missions” (四大志業)—charity, medical care, education, and humanistic culture—make it one of the world’s most influential Buddhist humanitarian networks. The “blue and white” volunteer uniform has become an international symbol of Taiwan’s civic compassion.

### Fo Guang Shan (佛光山): Humanistic Buddhism on a global scale

Established in 1967 by Master Hsing Yun (星雲大師), Fo Guang Shan champions a modern, socially engaged Buddhism. Its motto is clear: Buddhism should serve society, not retreat from it. With hundreds of temples worldwide and institutions like Fo Guang University and the Fo Guang Buddha Museum, it has shaped Taiwanese religious education and global outreach. Its emphasis on cultural production—publishing, art, and civic dialogue—reflects a deliberate strategy to modernize religious practice.

### Dharma Drum Mountain (法鼓山): modern Chan for modern minds

Founded by Master Sheng Yen (聖嚴法師) in 1989, Dharma Drum advocates “uplifting the character of humanity and building a pure land on earth.” It reframed Zen (Chan) meditation as a practice accessible to lay people and integrated it with modern psychology and education. Its academic work and digital archives have made it a crucial center for Buddhist scholarship.

### Chung Tai Chan Monastery (中台禪寺): tradition and contemporary aesthetics

Completed in 1994 under Master Wei Chueh (惟覺老和尚), Chung Tai blends strict Chan discipline with a modern architectural language. Its design and international outreach show how Taiwanese Buddhism has reimagined monastic life for a global era.

## Yiguandao (一貫道): a syncretic faith with strong social ties

Yiguandao, a tradition blending Confucian ethics, Buddhist compassion, and Daoist cosmology, arrived in Taiwan with postwar migrants. It operated underground for decades—classified as a “heterodox cult” in the 1950s–1980s—and was only legalized after the end of martial law. The history of suppression and survival helped forge a disciplined, tightly knit community.

Yiguandao’s emphasis on vegetarianism influenced Taiwan’s unusually high density of vegetarian restaurants. Its focus on family ethics and filial piety resonates with local values, while its global network—now spanning more than 80 countries—reflects Taiwan’s capacity for religious globalization.

## Christian traditions and local social engagement

### Presbyterian Church: faith intertwined with democracy

The Presbyterian Church in Taiwan (台灣基督長老教會) played a decisive role in human rights advocacy during the martial law era. Its statements on democracy and social justice positioned the church as a moral voice in Taiwan’s political transformation. It also advanced “indigenous theology,” articulating faith through Taiwan’s historical and cultural experience.

### Catholicism: education and care

Taiwan’s Catholic institutions—such as Fu Jen Catholic University and Mackay Memorial Hospital—have contributed significantly to education and healthcare. Foreign missionaries, especially in rural areas, left lasting infrastructure and social services that remain important today.

## Folk religion and the persistence of local ritual

Even as new movements grew, Taiwan’s folk religion remained vibrant. Mazu (媽祖) pilgrimages draw millions, becoming massive cultural festivals rather than purely devotional rites. Land God (土地公) shrines mark streets, markets, and office buildings, reflecting a grounded relationship between people and place. The Wangye (王爺) temple network in southern Taiwan remains a key system of community identity and annual ritual life.

These traditions function not only as belief systems but as social infrastructure: festivals organize communities, rituals preserve performing arts like glove puppetry and opera, and temple tourism sustains local economies.

## Religion as charity, civic energy, and social welfare

Taiwan’s religious NGOs professionalized volunteerism. They operate hospitals, schools, eldercare, and disaster response with a level of organization comparable to secular institutions. This has filled welfare gaps, especially in rural areas, and helped build a culture where civic service is seen as a spiritual practice.

## Modern challenges and adaptation

Religious groups have embraced digital platforms, online rituals, and social media—especially during the pandemic. Yet attracting younger generations remains a shared challenge. Many organizations now frame spiritual practice through environmental action, mental health, or cultural creativity to speak to new priorities.

## Global impact and “religious diplomacy”

Taiwan’s religious organizations offer a form of soft diplomacy. Their humanitarian work creates goodwill, their cultural programs promote Taiwan’s identity, and their global networks sustain unofficial international relationships—especially meaningful for a country with limited formal diplomatic recognition.

## Looking ahead

Taiwan’s spiritual culture is evolving toward more interfaith dialogue, social engagement, and international exchange. The island’s experience shows that religion can be a living civic force rather than a relic of tradition. For global audiences, Taiwan offers a compelling model: faith as public service, ritual as cultural memory, and compassion as a form of national character.
