# Taiwanese Baseball Culture: The National Sport Born of a Miracle

On August 25, 1968, in the remote township of Yanping, Taitung, a group of Indigenous children from a small school took their homemade wooden bats to face a star-studded Little League team from Japan. No one predicted that this David-and-Goliath match would end in a 7-0 victory, shocking the world. More importantly, no one could have known that this would be the spark that ignited Taiwan's baseball legend.

The victory of the **Hongye Little League** (紅葉少棒) team was more than just a win on the field; it was a small island's declaration of existence to the world. In an era when Taiwan faced diplomatic isolation and political setbacks, baseball became a vehicle for Taiwanese people to prove themselves and build a collective identity. From then on, this sport—originally an American import—took root in Taiwan, eventually becoming the "National Sport" (Guoqiu, 國球).

## Colonial Heritage and Cultural Transformation

Taiwanese baseball began during the Japanese colonial era. In 1906, the Taiwan Governor-General's High School (predecessor to Jian-guo High School) established the island's first baseball team. The Japanese brought more than just the rules of the game; they introduced a complete culture of discipline, teamwork, and "Spirit" (Seishin).

Ironically, baseball—originally introduced as a tool for "colonial civilization"—later became a medium for the Taiwanese people to assert their own identity. This cultural transformation is a microcosm of Taiwanese history: an ability to internalize foreign cultures and reinvent them with a unique local character.

## The Hongye Legend: A National Awakening

### Indigenous Dreams and Wooden Bats

The Hongye Elementary School team was composed primarily of Bunun Indigenous children. In 1960, when the team was founded, they practiced with homemade wooden bats and scavenged balls on a rocky, uneven field.

In 1963, Principal Lin Chu-peng noticed that student attendance was low. He came up with a simple idea: start a baseball team to give the children a reason to love coming to school. This modest goal unexpectedly launched Taiwan’s golden age of baseball.

### A Victory That Shook the World

On August 25, 1968, the Japanese Little League All-Star team visited Taiwan. Comprising elite players from the Kansai region, they expected an easy win. Instead, the Hongye children demonstrated incredible skill, winning 7-0 in the first game and repeating the feat the next day.

The result stunned the entire nation. Newspapers carried banner headlines, and the radio broadcasted the victory on repeat. Suddenly, the whole island knew about "Hongye" and the children who brought glory to Taiwan. Between 1975 and 1980, Chiang Ching-kuo visited Hongye four times, famously stating that "the whole world knows Free China has a Hongye Elementary School."

### World Champions: The Golden Dragon Team

Hongye's victory inspired the entire country. In 1969, the "Golden Dragon" (金龍) Little League team was formed to represent Taiwan at the Little League World Series in Williamsport, Pennsylvania. They swept the competition, taking home Taiwan's first world championship.

This title meant far more than sports. At a time when Taiwan had just left the United Nations and faced international isolation, these children made the world "see" Taiwan. Baseball was no longer just a sport; it was a way for Taiwan to have a voice on the global stage.

## The Golden Era of Youth Baseball

The success of the Golden Dragons launched a national fever. The government began promoting the "Three Levels" of youth baseball (Little League, Pony League, and Colt League). From the 1970s to the 1980s, Taiwan was a powerhouse in international youth baseball, frequently winning world titles. This period built a massive talent pool and a dedicated fan base, laying the foundation for professional baseball.

## The Birth of Professional Baseball (1990)

On March 17, 1990, the **CPBL** (Chinese Professional Baseball League, 中華職棒) played its first official game, entering its "Year One." The founding teams—Wei Chuan Dragons, Uni-President Lions, Mercuries Tigers, and Brother Elephants—became household names.

The establishment of the CPBL was a milestone, marking Taiwan's transition from amateur to professional sports. It also reflected Taiwan’s economic growth, showing the country had the financial strength to support a professional sports industry.

However, the CPBL faced severe challenges. In the late 1990s, several gambling scandals and game-fixing incidents severely damaged the league's image. Attendance plummeted, and several teams disbanded, leading to the darkest period in Taiwan's professional baseball history.

## International Success and the "Chien-Ming Wang Phenomenon"

### Olympic and Asian Games

Taiwan's national team, competing as **Chinese Taipei**, has consistently won medals at the Asian Games and the Olympics. These matches are not just competitions; they are emotional anchors for the Taiwanese people, often drawing the entire nation's attention.

### The Phenomenon of Chien-Ming Wang

In 2005, **Chien-Ming Wang** joined the New York Yankees, beginning a new chapter in Taiwanese baseball history. A right-handed pitcher from Tainan, his "Sinker" conquered MLB, leading the American League with 19 wins for two consecutive years (2006-2007).

Wang’s success was more than a personal achievement; it sparked a nationwide "baseball craze." Every day he started for the Yankees, Taiwan experienced a phenomenon of "everyone watching baseball," proving the unique position the sport holds in the hearts of the Taiwanese people.

## Why Baseball is the "National Sport"

In Taiwan, baseball is officially referred to as the "National Sport" (Guoqiu). This title carries deep political and cultural meaning. Scholars suggest that since 2000, the term "National Sport" has played a vital role in building a collective Taiwanese identity and a new national imagination.

Baseball provides a platform for a shared identity that transcends political divides. Regardless of political affiliation, Taiwanese people unite to cheer for the national team and take pride in their overseas stars. When we shout "Go, Taiwan!" at a stadium, we are not just supporting a team; we are confirming our own identity.

## Modern Challenges and New Horizons

### The Rebirth of the CPBL

After the low point of the gambling scandals, the CPBL has slowly rebuilt itself. The league implemented strict anti-gambling mechanisms, improved stadium facilities, and focused on enhancing game quality. New teams have joined, and the fan culture has become more mature and family-oriented.

### The Shohei Ohtani Effect and New Paradigms

In recent years, the success of Japanese star Shohei Ohtani in MLB has provided a new paradigm for Asian baseball. For Taiwan, Ohtani’s success offers a new direction for thinking: can we integrate more innovative training models and move beyond traditional frameworks?

### Women’s Baseball

Taiwanese women's baseball has also performed brilliantly on the international stage, achieving high rankings in the Women's Baseball World Cup. This growth represents a shift toward more diverse and inclusive sports development in Taiwan.

## Conclusion: The Eternal Dream

From the wooden bats of the Hongye Little League to the global stage of MLB, Taiwanese baseball has weathered more than half a century of ups and downs.

The sport is Taiwan's "National Sport" not just because of its success, but because it carries the dreams and emotions of millions. Baseball taught the Taiwanese people a vital lesson: even against powerful opponents and in the face of hardship, as long as we are united and persistent, we have a chance to create a miracle.

This is the essence of baseball, and the true meaning of the "National Sport."
