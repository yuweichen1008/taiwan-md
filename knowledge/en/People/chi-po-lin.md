# Chi Po-lin

On June 10, 2017, a helicopter crashed in Fengbin Township, Hualien, killing all three people on board. Among them was Chi Po-lin (齊柏林), a 52-year-old aerial photographer who was on his final flight, still conducting location scouting for "Beyond Beauty: Taiwan from Above II." This photographer, who spent 25 years documenting Taiwan's landscapes, ultimately became eternally united with the land he cherished.

## From Civil Servant to Aerial Photographer

Born in Taipei in 1964, Chi Po-lin entered Taipei City Government's Public Works Bureau after university graduation, becoming an ordinary civil servant. This stable job could have given him a comfortable, ordinary life, but fate arranged a different path for him in 1990.

That year, Chi needed to take a helicopter ride to survey Taipei city from above for work purposes. This experience completely changed his perception of Taiwan. Looking down from the sky, both Taiwan's beauty and sorrow appeared simultaneously: winding coastlines, majestic mountains, lush green farmlands, but also traces of urban expansion, scars of industrial pollution, and wounds from land development.

This shocking visual experience ignited a sense of mission deep within Chi Po-lin. He began to think: Why do we habitually view our land from a flat perspective, never truly "seeing" the full picture of Taiwan? From that moment, he decided to use his camera to record Taiwan's true face, allowing more people to see both the beauty and problems of this land.

Initially, this was just a hobby. Chi Po-lin used his spare time and personal funds to rent helicopters for aerial photography. Each flight was financially burdensome for him at the time, but he persisted. His wife once complained that he spent all their savings on "this hobby with no visible future," but Chi had a premonition that this work was more important than he imagined.

## 25 Years as an Aerial Chronicler

Starting in 1990, Chi Po-lin embarked on a 25-year Taiwan aerial documentation project. This wasn't a project with a clear endpoint, but a lifestyle choice. He visited almost every corner of Taiwan, from Fuguijiao (富貴角) in the north to Eluanbi (鵝鑾鼻) in the south, from the Pacific Ocean on the east coast to the Taiwan Strait on the west coast.

Chi's aerial photography wasn't random tourist shooting, but systematic documentation work. He would repeatedly photograph the same locations at different times and seasons, recording landscape changes. His lens captured the beauty of seasonal transitions and also documented changes caused by human development.

Over these 25 years, Chi Po-lin accumulated over 2,500 hours of flight time and shot hundreds of thousands of aerial photographs. These images constitute Taiwan's most complete aerial archive, recording this land's transformation process from the late 20th to early 21st century.

Each flight was an adventure for Chi Po-lin. The helicopter's noise, vibration, and turbulent airflow, combined with hours of concentrated photography, severely tested both body and spirit. Not to mention weather limitations and flight safety risks. But Chi never retreated, often saying: "Taiwan is so beautiful, it's worth dedicating my life to record it."

## The Shocking Debut of "Beyond Beauty: Taiwan from Above"

In 2011, Chi Po-lin made an important decision: he wanted to transform years of aerial photography into a documentary film, allowing more Taiwanese to truly "see Taiwan." This idea sounded simple, but execution proved extremely difficult.

First was the funding issue. Producing a high-quality documentary required substantial funds, but few were willing to invest in documentaries at the time, especially environmental documentaries. Chi sought sponsorship everywhere, facing countless rejections. Finally, Hou Hsiao-hsien (侯孝賢), owner of ARS Film Production, decided to support this project, enabling "Beyond Beauty: Taiwan from Above" to launch.

The filming process was harder than Chi anticipated. To achieve optimal shooting results, he and the photography team had to work under the most dangerous conditions: low-altitude flying, harsh weather, complex terrain. They once deployed immediately after a typhoon to record landscape changes caused by disasters; they also worked continuously for over ten hours in scorching heat, just to capture perfect lighting effects.

The production phase was equally challenging. How to transform static aerial images into dynamic film language? How to present Taiwan's beauty while also showing the severity of environmental problems? Chi and the post-production team repeatedly discussed and revised, striving for each shot to accurately convey his intended message.

## Historic Achievement as a National Documentary

In November 2013, "Beyond Beauty: Taiwan from Above" officially premiered. No one anticipated this documentary would achieve such remarkable success: nationwide box office exceeded NT$220 million, setting a historic high for Taiwan documentary box office. More importantly, the film won the Best Documentary Award at the 50th Golden Horse Awards, earning documentaries the respect they deserved.

The success of "Beyond Beauty: Taiwan from Above" was not just commercial, but cultural. In theaters, audiences saw their homeland from an aerial perspective for the first time, many moved to tears. The film's beautiful natural scenery was breathtaking, but images of industrial pollution, land abuse, and soil erosion were equally heartbreaking.

This documentary awakened Taiwan society's attention to environmental issues. The government began taking seriously environmental problems revealed by the aerial footage, and civil organizations intensified environmental protection actions. "Beyond Beauty: Taiwan from Above" wasn't just a movie; it became a catalyst for Taiwan's environmental consciousness awakening.

The film's international impact was equally stunning. "Beyond Beauty: Taiwan from Above" received acclaim at multiple international film festivals, allowing the world to see Taiwan's unique natural beauty and environmental challenges. Chi used his lens to introduce Taiwan to the world while reminding global audiences of environmental protection's importance.

## Environmental Mission Beyond Photography

The success of "Beyond Beauty: Taiwan from Above" transformed Chi Po-lin from an aerial photographer into an environmental protection spokesperson. He began receiving invitations to speak everywhere, sharing his discoveries and experiences during aerial photography. His words were always filled with deep affection for Taiwan's land and concern about environmental destruction.

Chi often said: "I'm not an environmentalist; I'm just a documenter. But when you see Taiwan's beauty and scars from above, you can't help being moved, and you can't help taking action." His aerial works became environmental groups' most powerful promotional materials—those shocking images were more persuasive than any words in explaining environmental protection's urgency.

Besides continuing aerial documentation, Chi also invested in environmental education work. He collaborated with schools, letting students understand their hometowns through aerial imagery, cultivating their emotional connection and sense of responsibility toward the land. He believed that only by letting more people truly "see" Taiwan could he inspire everyone's motivation to protect the environment.

## The Unfinished "Beyond Beauty: Taiwan from Above II"

The success of "Beyond Beauty: Taiwan from Above" led Chi Po-lin to decide on producing a sequel, "Beyond Beauty: Taiwan from Above II." If the first film was about "seeing problems," the second film's theme would be "seeing hope." He hoped to record Taiwan's efforts and achievements in environmental protection, showing people positive changes.

For this new project, Chi Po-lin once again embarked on aerial photography missions. In June 2017, he went to Hualien for location scouting for "Beyond Beauty: Taiwan from Above II." During those days, his work enthusiasm remained as high as always, full of anticipation for the upcoming new filming.

On the morning of June 10, Chi Po-lin took off from Hualien Airport in a helicopter, preparing to photograph coastal terrain around Hualien's Fengbin area. This was routine work he had performed countless times, but this time, fate dealt him a cruel ending.

## Eternal Aerial Poet

Chi Po-lin's death in service shocked all of Taiwan society. A documenter who had dedicated 25 years of his youth to Taiwan ultimately sacrificed his life doing the work he loved most. His death made many realize that some people love this land with their lives.

Chi Po-lin left behind not just hundreds of thousands of aerial photographs and one successful documentary, but more importantly, a spiritual legacy. He proved through action that ordinary people, with sufficient passion and persistence, can create enormous value for society.

His aerial works continue to exhibit worldwide, continuing to tell Taiwan's story to people. "Beyond Beauty: Taiwan from Above" has also become one of the most important documentaries in Taiwan film history, changing how Taiwanese view their own land and elevating Taiwan documentaries' international status.

"Beyond Beauty: Taiwan from Above II" was eventually completed through other directors' efforts. Although Chi Po-lin couldn't see the final result, his spirit was deeply imprinted in this work. Whenever the film plays, audiences can feel his deep love for Taiwan.

## Flying Memories, Immortal Images

Chi Po-lin's life can be divided into two phases: the ordinary years before becoming an aerial photographer, and the subsequent 27 years of documentation career. But what truly defined him was how he chose to spend this life. He chose a path full of risks but meaningful, using his lens to leave precious visual memories for Taiwan.

His story tells us that love can enable a person to do extraordinary things. Chi Po-lin's love for Taiwan was pure, profound, and unconditional. This love made him willing to endure physical exhaustion, economic pressure, family misunderstanding, and ultimately pay with his life.

Today, when we fly over Taiwan, when we admire aerial photography online, when we discuss environmental protection, we should remember Chi Po-lin. He spent 25 years teaching Taiwanese one thing: to truly love a place, you must first truly see it.

Chi Po-lin has departed, but his eyes still fly in the sky, his lens still records Taiwan's changes. Between every cloud, in every play of light and shadow, there is his endless gaze upon this land.
