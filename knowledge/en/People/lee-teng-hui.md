# Lee Teng-hui

Lee Teng-hui (李登輝, 1923–2020) is one of the most consequential figures in modern Taiwanese history. As Taiwan’s first Taiwan-born president, he oversaw a peaceful transition from authoritarian rule to democracy between 1988 and 2000—a transformation often described as a **“quiet revolution.”** International observers dubbed him **“Mr. Democracy,”** reflecting his central role in shaping Taiwan’s democratic institutions and political identity.

Lee’s life spanned multiple eras: Japanese colonial rule, World War II, the arrival of the Nationalist government, martial law, and finally democratization. This layered historical experience informed his political worldview and gave him a unique legitimacy across generations.

## From Scholar to Statesman

Lee’s early path was academic. He studied at Taipei Higher School and the agricultural department of Taihoku Imperial University. After the war, he pursued graduate studies in the United States and earned a Ph.D. in agricultural economics from Cornell University in 1968. At the time, this international academic background was rare among Taiwanese leaders, and it shaped his global outlook.

Returning to Taiwan, he worked in the Joint Commission on Rural Reconstruction (JCRR), then moved into public service: provincial government roles, mayor of Taipei, governor of Taiwan Province, and eventually vice president. When President Chiang Ching-kuo passed away in 1988, Lee assumed the presidency—an apparently constitutional succession that opened a historic window for democratic reform.

## Constitutional Reform and the End of Authoritarian Rule

Lee’s most enduring legacy is political reform. Under his leadership, Taiwan dismantled key pillars of authoritarianism:

- **Martial law was lifted**, expanding civil liberties.
- **Party and press restrictions were loosened**, enabling a vibrant public sphere.
- **Six rounds of constitutional amendments** reshaped the political system to reflect Taiwan’s reality rather than mainland representation.

One of the most symbolic breakthroughs was the dissolution of the “ten-thousand-year parliament.” In 1991, the first generation of lifelong legislators—elected on the mainland in 1947 and retained for decades—retired. New elections created a legislature that truly represented Taiwan’s people.

In 1996, Taiwan held its **first direct presidential election**, and Lee won with a strong mandate. This moment marked the completion of Taiwan’s democratic transition and placed the island among the world’s successful cases of peaceful democratization.

## Redefining Cross-Strait Relations

Lee also reshaped how Taiwan articulated itself internationally. In 1995, he delivered a speech at Cornell University—his alma mater—where he highlighted Taiwan’s democratic achievements and distinct political trajectory. In 1999, he advanced the “special state-to-state relations” concept, arguing that Taiwan and China should be seen as two separate political entities.

These positions were controversial, drawing criticism from Beijing and within Taiwan. Yet they also reflected a growing sense of **Taiwanese subjectivity**—a belief that Taiwan’s identity and political future should be defined by its own democratic society.

## A Complex Political Legacy

Lee’s legacy remains contested. To supporters, he is the **father of Taiwan’s democracy**, the leader who dismantled authoritarianism and made direct elections possible. To critics, he fractured the Kuomintang and deepened cross-strait tensions. These debates reflect broader divisions in Taiwan’s historical memory and political identity.

Regardless of political stance, his role in democratization is widely acknowledged. Lee demonstrated that a Chinese-speaking society could achieve a peaceful democratic transition—offering a model for other authoritarian contexts.

## The Intellectual in Power

Lee was not only a politician but also a thinker. He frequently referenced philosophy and history in his speeches and writings, and after retirement he continued publishing essays and books on Taiwan’s political future. His intellectual style contrasted with the image of purely technocratic leadership and gave his presidency a distinct tone.

## Enduring Significance

Lee Teng-hui passed away in 2020 at the age of 97. His death marked the end of an era, but the institutions he helped build remain central to Taiwan’s political life. In a time when global democracy faces increasing challenges, Taiwan’s democratic resilience stands out—and Lee’s reforms are a major reason why.

For many Taiwanese, Lee represents more than a political leader; he embodies the transition from a controlled state to a citizen-driven society. The “quiet revolution” he led continues to shape Taiwan’s identity, both domestically and on the international stage.
