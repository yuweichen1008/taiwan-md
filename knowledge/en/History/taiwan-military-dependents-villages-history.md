# Taiwan Military Dependents' Villages History

A bamboo fence enclosed half a century of nostalgia. From temporary settlement in 1949 to today's cultural heritage sites, military dependents' villages (juancun 眷村) witnessed Mainlander immigrants taking root in Taiwan, recording a generation's identity transformation from "retaking the mainland" to becoming "Taiwanese."

## Early Post-War Period: Temporary but Permanent Settlement

### 1949: The Hasty Great Migration

In 1949, as the Chinese Civil War concluded in defeat, approximately 1.2 million military personnel and civilians relocated to Taiwan with the ROC government. About 600,000 were soldiers, with another 600,000 being military families and civil servants. This great migration was one of the largest population movements in modern history.

The government originally believed this was merely temporary retreat, expecting to soon "retake the mainland." Therefore, military family settlement adopted temporary measures. Near military camps or city outskirts, simple housing was hastily constructed for immediate family placement. These temporary buildings became the earliest prototypes of military dependents' villages.

The first villages were mostly built with local materials - bamboo, thatch, and earth bricks. Houses were small and cramped, with families of several members often sharing a single room. Without running water or sewage systems, living conditions were extremely harsh. But for Mainlanders in exile, this represented precious shelter.

### Hardship Years: Emotional Bonds Through Shared Suffering

The 1950s military villages resembled small "Chinatowns." Mainlanders from across China united due to shared circumstances. Shandong strongmen, Jiangnan ladies, Sichuan veterans, and northeastern youth all crowded into cramped villages.

Village spatial design reflected this collectivity. Rows of connected bungalows with shared kitchens, toilets, and laundry areas necessitated close resident interaction. Neighbors had no privacy but also no distance. Children played in alleys, women chatted while washing clothes at wells, and veterans played chess and shared stories under large banyan trees.

The "hardship philosophy" (kenan zhexue 克難哲學) best embodied village spirit. In materially scarce times, villagers developed remarkable creativity. Empty oil drums became water tanks, waste wood boards built houses, and broken umbrellas became awnings. This "creating something from nothing" capability became a crucial characteristic of village culture.

## 1960s-70s: Islands of Culture

### Formation of "Little China"

As hopes for "retaking the mainland" gradually dimmed, villages began transitioning from temporary settlement to permanent residence. The government began systematically rebuilding villages, providing better living conditions. Simultaneously, this formed "Little Chinas" isolated from local society.

Villages had their own schools, markets, clinics, and activity centers. Village children received "Chinese cultural education" from childhood, speaking standard Mandarin, reading classical texts, learning calligraphy and painting. Their understanding of the "motherland" often exceeded their knowledge of Taiwan - they could recite Yangtze River basin place names but didn't know the neighboring stream's name.

Villages also maintained strong military characteristics. Military rank determined housing size, and veterans maintained connections after retirement. During National Day, Youth Day, and other important holidays, villages held flag-raising ceremonies demonstrating ROC loyalty.

### Formation of Provincial Origin Divisions

Village insularity gradually created provincial origin divisions. Mainlanders primarily lived in villages while Taiwanese rarely entered. The two communities developed separately, lacking deep exchange. Language, cultural, and political differences invisibly erected barriers.

These divisions were particularly evident in educational systems. Village children attended military schools or Mandarin model schools, while Taiwanese children entered regular public schools. Different educational environments cultivated different cultural identities.

Political differences also gradually emerged. Mainlanders supported the KMT and identified with the ROC, while Taiwanese gradually developed Taiwan consciousness, questioning external rule. These political divisions evolved into intense conflicts during later democratization.

## 1980s: Change and Differentiation

### Economic Miracle's Impact

Taiwan's rapid economic development in the 1980s continuously increased urbanization. Originally suburban villages suddenly found themselves in prime locations. Land prices soared, surrounding high-rises proliferated, making villages' low bungalows incongruous.

Economically improved village families began leaving villages, purchasing modern apartments. Those remaining were mostly families with poorer economic conditions. Villages began experiencing class differentiation as original community consciousness gradually disintegrated.

Simultaneously, outsiders began entering villages. Due to cheap rent, some Taiwanese, foreign spouses, and foreign workers began renting in villages. Villages' "pure Mainlander" characteristics gradually faded as population composition became complex.

### Identity Crisis and Cultural Preservation

With political martial law lifting and cross-strait opening, villagers faced unprecedented identity crises. "Retaking the mainland" was no longer possible, "homeland" became a place for family visits, and "Taiwan" transformed from temporary residence to permanent home.

First-generation Mainlanders, while still nostalgic for home, accepted the reality of ending their lives in Taiwan. Second-generation village children began reflecting on their identity: Am I Chinese or Taiwanese? Is the village my home or historical baggage?

This identity anxiety catalyzed village cultural preservation movements. Some village children began recording village history, preserving old photographs, and interviewing elderly residents. They realized villages carried not only personal memories but important components of Taiwan's multicultural heritage.

## Reconstruction and Disappearance: Urban Renewal Waves

### 1990s: Large-Scale Reconstruction

Beginning in the 1990s, the government promoted the "Military Dependents' Villages Reconstruction Act," planning comprehensive reconstruction of aging villages. This policy aimed to improve village living quality while releasing land for urban development.

Reconstructed villages became modernized public housing with significantly improved living conditions. But original settlement patterns disappeared, and neighborhood relationships became distant. Villagers moving into new apartments enjoyed modern conveniences but lost the intimate community feeling.

Reconstruction wasn't smooth sailing. Some village residents refused relocation, were dissatisfied with allocation results, causing many disputes. While the government provided compensation, it couldn't remedy emotional loss. Village disappearance meant the end of a lifestyle.

### Cultural Heritage Awakening

Facing rapid village disappearance, cultural preservation voices began rising. Scholars, cultural historians, and village children joined forces, demanding preservation of historically valuable village buildings.

In 1999, Xinyi Public Assembly Hall (formerly Sisi South Village) became Taipei's first village designated as historic architecture. This decision held symbolic significance, representing society's beginning recognition of village cultural value.

Subsequently, villages across Taiwan were designated as cultural heritage sites. Treasure Hill (Baozangyan), Rainbow Village (Caihong juancun), and Jianye New Village all received preservation due to unique historical significance or artistic value. These surviving villages became important windows for understanding village culture.

## Diverse Facets of Village Culture

### Cuisine Culture: Flavors from Across China

One of the most representative village cultural legacies is cuisine. Village mothers from across China recreated hometown flavors in simple kitchens. Shandong steamed buns, Sichuan braised pork, Jiangsu-Zhejiang soup dumplings, and Hunan stir-fried pork with peppers - regional flavors converged and merged in villages.

Due to limited resources, village cuisine developed a unique "hardship style." One stew pot could feed the whole family, one piece of tofu could be prepared multiple ways. These simple home-style dishes later became representatives of Taiwan's "Mainland cuisine."

Village night markets were also important sources of Taiwan's night market culture. Small stalls behind bamboo fences, starting with a few tables and chairs, gradually developed into bustling markets. Many famous food stalls originated from villages.

### Language Culture: Mandarin Localization

Villages were crucial bases for promoting Mandarin. Here, Mainlanders from different provinces had to communicate in Mandarin, with children speaking standard Mandarin from childhood. Villages could be considered pioneers of Taiwan's Mandarin-ization.

But village Mandarin also absorbed Taiwanese influences. "Hao hao chi" (very delicious), "You gou" (really), "Hen li hai" (very impressive) - these Taiwan-style Mandarin expressions emerged in village multicultural environments. Language fusion reflected cultural exchange.

Village children performed particularly well in literature, drama, and film. Pai Hsien-yung, Chu T'ien-wen, Chu T'ien-hsin, Hou Hsiao-hsien all had village backgrounds. Their works deeply portrayed village life while enriching Taiwan's cultural content.

### Music and Entertainment: Military Songs and Popular Music's Resonance

Village musical culture was extremely rich. Military songs were the most representative musical form - "Plum Blossom," "Night Raid," "Nine Good Men in One Squad" - continuously sung in villages. These songs weren't merely entertainment but carried political identity and emotional memory.

But villages were also cradles of Taiwan's popular music. Many famous singers had village backgrounds: Teresa Teng, Fei Yu-ching, Chang Ti, Pan Yueyun. Their musical styles fused mainland folk songs, Taiwanese songs, and Western music, forming Taiwan's unique popular music style.

Film culture was also developed in villages. Open-air movies were important village entertainment - a white sheet hung on a wall became a simple cinema. Adults and children brought chairs, gathering to watch movies with collective viewing pleasure incomparable to modern theaters.

## Generational Memory Transmission and Rupture

### First Generation: Eternal Nostalgia

The village first generation - Mainlanders who came to Taiwan in 1949 - maintained attachment to their homeland. They rebuilt "small homelands" in villages, maintaining original lifestyle habits. Language, food, festivals, and interpersonal relationships maintained "Chinese flavor" as much as possible.

But time spares no one, and first-generation villagers gradually passed away. They took not only personal memories but witness to an era. Few first-generation survivors remain today, with their stories becoming precious historical materials.

### Second Generation: Struggling with Identity

Village second generation faced complex identity issues. They grew up in villages, received Chinese cultural education, but their growth land was Taiwan. With political martial law lifting and social opening, they began questioning their identity positioning.

Some chose to embrace Taiwan identity, considering Taiwan their homeland; others insisted on Chinese cultural identity, emphasizing their "Chinese" identity. More navigated between identities, forming "dual identity" or "multiple identities."

This identity anxiety was also reflected politically. Village children's political positions weren't monolithic - some supported unification, others independence, with many supporting status quo maintenance. Political position diversification reflected internal divisions within this ethnic group.

### Third Generation: Village Memory Dilution

Village third generation mostly didn't grow up in villages, with village memories mainly from parental narratives. Their Taiwan identity was stronger, with more superficial understanding of Chinese culture.

Interestingly, some third generation developed intense interest in village culture. Through fieldwork and oral history, they re-explored villages. This "cultural root-seeking" phenomenon reflected people's desire for cultural origins in the globalization era.

## Contemporary Villages: From Disappearance to Rebirth

### New Life Through Cultural Creative Industries

Recently, some preserved villages gained new life. Through cultural creative industry introduction, villages transformed from declining communities to cultural parks. Huashan Cultural Creative Park and Songshan Cultural Creative Park, while not villages, inherited village transformation concepts.

The successful case of actual village transformation is Kaohsiung's "Live-in Conservation" program. The government attracted young people to live in old villages with low rent, with conditions of assisting heritage maintenance and community development participation. This program revitalized old villages while training young cultural historians.

Rainbow Village is another success case. Grandpa Huang Yongfu's colorful murals, originally protest actions against demolition, accidentally became famous tourist attractions. Rainbow Village now attracts millions of annual visitors, becoming Taichung's cultural landmark.

### Memory Preservation in the Digital Age

Digital technology provided new tools for village cultural preservation. Through 3D scanning and VR technology, disappeared villages can be rebuilt in virtual spaces. "Digital Village Museums" allow younger generations to experience village life immersively.

Oral history projects also extensively use digital technology. Through video recording, village elders' stories are completely preserved. These precious historical materials serve not only as academic research materials but as testimony to Taiwan's multicultural heritage.

Social media also became new platforms for village emotional connections. Facebook groups like "XX Village Alumni Association" and "Village Children Fellowship" allow scattered villagers to rebuild connections. Online virtual communities, to some extent, recreate physical village community feelings.

## Contemporary Significance of Village Spirit

Reviewing village history, we see not only one ethnic group's story but an important chapter in Taiwan's multicultural formation. Villages demonstrated cultural exchange and fusion on Taiwan's land, witnessing foreign ethnic groups' localization processes.

Village spirit's core is "hardship resilience" (kenan) and "mutual assistance." In materially scarce times, villagers developed tenacious vitality and close community relationships. This spirit still holds important inspirational significance in today's individualistic society.

Villages' multicultural characteristics also provided valuable assets for Taiwan society. Cultures from across China converged here, producing rich literature, art, and musical works. These cultural achievements don't belong to specific ethnic groups but are collective wealth for all Taiwanese.

More importantly, village experience reminds us that cultural identity isn't a zero-sum game. One person can simultaneously be a village child, Taiwanese, and Chinese - these identities don't exclude each other. In the globalization era, we need exactly this kind of open, inclusive, multiple identity.

Villages may have disappeared, but village spirit exists forever. It reminds us to cherish community emotions, respect cultural differences, and maintain resilience through change. What bamboo fences enclosed wasn't just a community, but precious memories of Taiwan's multiculturalism.
