# New Media Art in Taiwan: Digital Creation on an Island

Deep in the azure of the Pacific, there is an island whose heart beats with the rhythm of semiconductors and whose veins flow with the brilliance of fiber optics. This island is not only a hub in the global technology supply chain but also a unique artistic laboratory—where millennia-old Eastern aesthetics meet contemporary digital technology, giving birth to a world-renowned new media art wave.

From experimental explorations in the 1980s to shining on international stages such as the Venice Biennale and Art Basel Miami today, Taiwan's new media art has carved out a path of its own. This is not simple technical exhibition but a profound poetic creation about "how to maintain humanity in the digital flood."

## Artistic Genes of a Tech Island

The uniqueness of Taiwan's new media art comes first from the special cultural genes of this island. When the semiconductor industry made Taiwan a "Tech Island," artists were not kidnapped by technical rationality; instead, they learned a rare skill: **letting technology serve emotion, letting algorithms carry poetry**.

This unique technical humanism allows Taiwan's new media art to present a face quite different from Europe, America, Japan, and Korea. Western new media art often emphasizes the avant-garde nature and criticality of concepts, while Japan and Korea lean towards refined technical aesthetics. Taiwan's new media art finds a warm and vital expression between the implicit poetry of the East and modern technology.

As demonstrated in artist Wu Che-yu's solo exhibition _Formula of All Things_ (萬物公式) at Taipei 101: algorithms are not cold mathematical symbols but organic life forms that can grow, breathe, and evolve. The digital creatures he created with p5.js swim in massive commercial spaces, as if telling an ancient story—about how life sprouts in the most unlikely places.

## Policy Soil and Institutional Nutrients

The vigorous development of Taiwan's new media art is inseparable from the government's forward-looking layout. From the Ministry of Culture's "Cultural Technology Development Project" to the National Culture and Arts Foundation's "Tech-Art Creation Project," policymakers have shown a rare cultural vision—they understand that in the era of AI and virtual reality, art is not a decoration for technology but an important weapon for humans facing the future.

The establishment of **C-LAB (Taiwan Contemporary Culture Lab)** is the best embodiment of this policy vision. This cultural laboratory, transformed from the former Air Force General Headquarters, is a metaphorical existence in itself—from military defense to cultural creation, from a closed martial law site to an open creative base. Here, artists have the most advanced equipment, the most cutting-edge technical support, and more importantly, an environment that truly supports experimentation and failure.

C-LAB's "dialog() Asia New Media Art Festival" has become one of the most important digital art events in Asia, attracting artists, curators, and technical experts from all over the world every year. It's not just a place to display works but a field for intellectual collision and a laboratory for the fusion of Eastern and Western artistic concepts.

## Representative Artists' Creative Universes

### Lin Pey-chwen: Poet of Life Science

Lin Pey-chwen is a pioneer in Taiwan's digital art. Her _Eve Clone_ (夏娃克隆) series of works explored the complex relationships between genetic engineering, artificial life, and the female body long before biotechnology became a hot topic. Her work is not simple praise or criticism of technology, but a deeper philosophical reflection: when life can be copied and transformed, what is the true "I"?

In her interactive installations, viewers often need to drive visual and sound changes through biological signals (heartbeat, breathing, brainwaves). This design hides her profound understanding of the "body as medium." Technology here is not an external tool but an internal extension.

### Huang Hsin-chien: Poetry of Virtual Reality

While most people still treat VR as a gaming toy, Huang Hsin-chien was already thinking about how to use this medium to tell stories. His VR work _La Camera Insabbiata_ (Chalkroom), a collaboration with legendary American musician Laurie Anderson, won the Best VR Experience at the 2017 Venice Film Festival—this was not just a personal honor but an important milestone for Taiwan's new media art on the international stage.

The charm of _Chalkroom_ lies in its complete subversion of the stereotype of VR as a "tool for escaping reality." In this virtual space, viewers experience a profound meditation on memory, death, and rebirth. Huang uses digital technology to create a Zen space that transcends physical limitations, where Eastern views on life and death perfectly merge with Western experimental art.

### LuxuryLogico: Mechanical Poetics and Collective Creation

LuxuryLogico (豪華朗機工) is perhaps Taiwan's most influential cross-disciplinary art collective. Composed of artists Chang Geng-hao, Chang Geng-hua, Lin Kun-ying, and Chen Yi, the collective is known for "mechanical poetics," their works often combining precise engineering technology with emotional aesthetic expression.

The cauldron for the 2017 Taipei Summer Universiade is LuxuryLogico's most well-known work. This 21-meter-tall mechanical flower bloomed slowly during the opening ceremony, perfectly combining Taiwan's technological strength with humanistic spirit. At that moment, the world saw the energy of Taiwan's new media art—the elegance of Eastern flowers, the precision of modern machinery, and the warmth of collective creation.

Their _The Sound of Blooming_ (聆聽花開的聲音) at the Taichung World Flora Exposition, consisting of 697 mechanical flowers, each opening or closing according to changes in ambient sound. This work transformed the abstract concept of "dialogue between technology and nature" into a concrete and perceptible poetic experience.

### Wu Che-yu: Life Poetics of Algorithms

Among the younger generation of new media artists in Taiwan, Wu Che-yu is a special presence. He is a code artist and a poet; he is familiar with every function of p5.js and also deeply versed in the rhythms and beats of music. His generative art work _SoulFish_ swam at the Venice Biennale, and _The Soul of Flowers_ (花的靈魂) bloomed at Art Basel Miami, each work telling the same story: how digital technology carries the temperature of life.

In Wu Che-yu's creative philosophy, algorithms are not tools for controlling life but the language for life to express itself. His works often have a certain "digital vitality"—they grow, change, and evolve, and each presentation is unique. This creative method contains profound Eastern philosophical thinking: everything has a spirit, including those digital existences composed of code.

His solo exhibition _Formula of All Things_ at Taipei 101 transformed a commercial space into a massive digital ecosystem. When viewers walk past, the creatures on the screen change their behavior patterns due to the presence of people, as if they truly have the ability to perceive. This "human-machine empathy" experience design reflects the humanistic care unique to Taiwan's new media art.

### Wang Fu-rui: Archaeologist of Sound

Wang Fu-rui is a pioneer of sound art in Taiwan, his works often exploring the complex relationships between sound and space, memory and time. In his installations, technology is not for creating novel sounds but for excavating forgotten auditory experiences and for teaching us how to "listen" again.

His work _Beyond the Sound_ (失聲祭), through special sound processing technology, reproduces those nearly disappearing traditional Taiwanese music and dialects. This creative orientation reflects an important feature of Taiwan's new media art: deep concern for local culture. Technology here is not a symbol of Westernization but a tool for preserving and passing on cultural memory.

### Yao Chung-han: Musician of Light and Shadow

Yao Chung-han's sound and light art works perfectly combine the temporality of music with the spatiality of vision. He is skilled at creating synesthetic experiences that allow viewers to "see sound" and "hear light" simultaneously. In his installations, LED strips, sensors, and speakers are no longer cold electronic components but living entities that can breathe and sing.

His _Radiant Beast_ (光電獸) series transforms the beats of electronic music into a dance of light, creating a new "light-sound aesthetic." This creative method embodies a common trait of Taiwanese new media artists: not satisfied with single-medium expression but wanting to create a holistic, cross-sensory experience.

## Important Exhibitions and Cultural Events

### Digital Art Festival Taipei: Annual Grand Event

Since it began in 2006, the Digital Art Festival Taipei has become an annual carnival of digital art. This festival is not just a platform for displaying works but a field for exchanging ideas. Every year's theme reflects the Taipei digital art community's keen observation of current social issues.

From early discussions on "digital aesthetics" to recent explorations of "AI art" and "sustainable technology," the Digital Art Festival Taipei has always stood at the forefront of the times, providing a platform for artists and audiences to think about the future. The 2025 theme "Infinite Horizon" embodies Taiwan's profound thinking about the concept of "boundaries" as an island nation.

### IRCAM Forum Taipei: East-West Exchange

The forum established by France's IRCAM (Institute for Research and Coordination in Acoustics/Music) in Taipei has brought international-level resources and vision to Taiwan's sound art and music technology. This platform not only introduces advanced Western technology but, more importantly, creates space for a dialogue between Eastern and Western musical philosophies.

Here, Taiwanese artists have the opportunity to exchange ideas with top international music technology experts while also showcasing the unique charm of Eastern musical aesthetics. This two-way cultural exchange is an important path for the internationalization of Taiwan's new media art.

### Taiwan Pavilion at Venice Biennale: International Stage

Taiwan's journey in the Venice Biennale witnesses the development trajectory of Taiwan's new media art from the periphery to the mainstream. From early displays primarily of traditional painting and sculpture to the large-scale presentation of new media and tech-art works in recent years, the change in the curation direction of the Taiwan Pavilion reflects the international art world's recognition of Taiwan's tech-art.

The exhibition of Huang Hsin-chien's VR works and Wu Che-yu's generative art works in this highest-standard international art palace marks that Taiwan's new media art has the strength to engage in equal dialogue with the Euro-American art world.

## Generative Art: Taiwan's Digital Genesis

On the global map of generative art, Taiwan occupies a unique and important position. This is not only because Taiwan has a strong pool of programming talent but also because Taiwanese artists have shown different philosophical thinking when dealing with the core issue of "human-machine collaboration."

### Taiwanese Voices on fxHash and Art Blocks

On decentralized NFT art platforms, works by Taiwanese artists often appear on collectors' watchlists. Wu Che-yu's _SoulFish_ series on fxHash gives each digital fish a unique "DNA," and they generate different forms and behavior patterns according to the randomness of the algorithm. This creative method hides profound philosophical thinking: in the digital world, what is "individuality"? What is "the uniqueness of life"?

Taiwanese generative artists are not satisfied with pure visual aesthetics; they are more concerned with how to inject emotion, memory, and even spirituality into algorithms. This creative orientation makes Taiwanese generative art present a warm and humanistic quality.

### Eastern Interpretation of Algorithmic Aesthetics

While Western generative artists mostly focus on the purity of algorithms and the avant-garde nature of concepts, Taiwanese artists tend to view algorithms as a kind of "Tao"—a natural law of operation, a way for life to show itself.

In Wu Che-yu's creation, we can see this Eastern algorithmic aesthetic: his digital creatures are not mechanical repetitions but organic growth; not pre-set animations but real-time generations; not artificial fabrications but natural manifestations. This creative philosophy perfectly combines ancient Eastern views of nature with modern computational science.

## Future Outlook: Artistic Imagination in the AI Era

When ChatGPT and various AI tools began to reshape human creative methods, Taiwanese new media artists did not feel panic but instead saw new possibilities. They understand that AI is not to replace human creativity but to form a new partnership with human creativity.

### Taiwanese Experiments in AI Art

In C-LAB's AI Art Lab, artists are exploring various possibilities of artificial intelligence and creative expression. These experiments are not to prove the power of AI but to explore how human creativity evolves and redefines itself in the AI era.

Taiwan's AI art experiments show obvious "symbiotic" characteristics—not letting AI create completely autonomously, nor letting humans completely control AI, but creating a "human-machine dance" creative model. In this model, AI provides an infinite possibility space, while humans provide value judgment and emotional guidance.

### Poetic Applications of XR (Extended Reality)

When Taiwanese artists apply XR technology, they rarely purely pursue dazzling technical effects but are more concerned with how to use these technologies to create new narrative possibilities and new modes of emotional experience.

Huang Hsin-chien's VR work is the best example—he is not showing off the immersion of VR but exploring the possibility of VR as a "meditation tool." In his virtual space, what viewers experience is not the pleasure of escaping reality but the courage to face the inner self.

## Hub for Digital Art in Asia

Taiwan's position in the Asian new media art landscape comes not only from her technical strength but also from her unique cultural position. As an island where Eastern and Western cultures meet, Taiwan possesses a rare "cultural translation ability"—able to both understand Western rational logic and appreciate Eastern emotional wisdom.

### Differentiated Development from Japan and Korea

Compared to the refinement of Japanese new media art and the commercialization of Korean new media art, Taiwan's new media art focuses more on "humanistic concern" and "experimental spirit." This differentiated development path allows Taiwan to occupy a unique ecological niche in the Asian new media art ecosystem.

Taiwanese artists are good at finding a balance between experimentalism and accessibility. Their works have both avant-garde technical explorations and warm humanistic care. This quality gives Taiwan's new media art high recognition on the international stage.

## Conclusion: Island Writing of Digital Poetics

The development history of Taiwan's new media art is actually a history of creation about "how to maintain poetry in the technological era." From Yuan Goang-ming's video poetics to Huang Hsin-chien's VR meditation, from LuxuryLogico's mechanical garden to Wu Che-yu's algorithmic life forms, Taiwanese new media artists have always been exploring the same proposition: how to let cold technology carry warm humanity?

This small island answered an important era question with her creative practice: technological progress and humanistic concern are not opposite; on the contrary, the most profound technical innovations often come from the most sincere humanistic concerns. When we learn to write poetry with algorithms, compose music with code, and convey love with digital media, we find the way to be human in the digital age.

In the global tide of technology, Taiwan's new media art, with its unique "warm technology" concept, provides the world with a new possibility: technology can be poetic, digital can be warm, and artificial intelligence can be loving. This is perhaps the greatest gift of Taiwan's new media art to the world—it reminds us that no matter how technology develops, the core of art is always about love, about connection, about finding eternity in change.

On this small island in the Pacific, new media art is not just an art form but a way of life, a worldview, and a gentle imagination of the future. While the world is anxious about whether AI will replace humans, Taiwanese artists are already telling us the answer with their works: the true future is not humans being replaced by machines, but humans and machines creating a better world together.
