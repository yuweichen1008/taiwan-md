# Taiwanese Photography: Light and Shadow of the Island

The development of photography in Taiwan is like an epic poem of light and shadow on the island. From the establishment of the first photo studios during the Japanese colonial era to the rise of documentary photography after the war, and then to the diverse innovations of contemporary photographic art, Taiwanese photography not only records the changes of this land but also presents the aesthetics, cultural memory, and deep attachment of the Taiwanese people to their home under the lens of different eras. These images are not just visual records; they are witnesses of the times, interpreters of culture, and weavers of collective memory.

## Colonial Gaze and Local Awakening: The Germination of Taiwanese Photography

### The Introduction of Photographic Technology and Colonial Investigations

The emergence of photography in Taiwan is closely related to the beginning of Japanese colonial rule. After the First Sino-Japanese War in 1895, Japan obtained rule over Taiwan and immediately sent military photographers to record the conquest process. Taiwan became an important field for early war photography practice. Although these images have a strong colonial color, they are the earliest systematic photographic records of Taiwan, opening the prelude to the history of Taiwanese photography.

The popularity of dry-plate photography in the 1880s made photography relatively easy and portable, no longer requiring heavy darkroom equipment and chemicals. This technological advancement became an important tool for the Japanese colonial government to appoint anthropologists and geographers to conduct field investigations. Scholars like **Torii Ryūzō** (鳥居龍藏) and **Mori Ushinosuke** (森丑之助) brought cameras deep into Taiwan's high mountains to measure and record the Indigenous peoples, leaving behind precious visual data.

### The Rise of Photo Studios and Local Photographic Talent

Starting from the 1920s, local Taiwanese youth began to learn photographic techniques. They acquired skills by studying abroad in Japan or working as apprentices in photo studios and subsequently opened studios for business. These early Taiwanese photographers not only mastered basic skills but also delved into deeper photographic aesthetics.

According to records, the earliest photo studio opened by a Taiwanese was "Erwo Photo Studio" (二我寫真館), founded by Lukang native Shi Qiang in 1901. In the genealogy of Hakka photographers, the Jiang family of Beipu also played an important role. Jiang Zhen-xiang, the son of the pioneer Jiang Shao-zu, was the first person in Beipu to own a camera and was the uncle of the later famous photographer **Deng Nan-guang** (鄧南光). The Jiang family still preserves glass plate negatives from the 1890s, recording family portraits in front of Tianshui Hall.

### From Pictorial Salon to Shinko Shashin (New Photography)

Taiwanese photography during the Japanese era was initially influenced by the mainstream Japanese "Artistic Photography" (Pictorial Salon Photography). This style pursues pictorial beauty and emphasizes composition and mood. However, with the development of theory and equipment, some enthusiasts began to contact the more avant-garde "Shinko Shashin" (新興寫真術, _xīnxīng xiězhēn shù_).

Shinko Shashin emphasizes "realistic observation of street hunting," advocating that photography should directly face reality and record true living conditions rather than deliberately creating beautified images. The introduction of this concept injected the spirit of realism into Taiwanese photography, laying the foundation for the later development of documentary photography.

## Exploration of Modernity: Chang Chao-tang's Experimental Images

### The Birth of the Father of Taiwanese Photography

In the history of Taiwanese photography, **Chang Chao-tang** (張照堂, 1943–2024) is undoubtedly one of the most revolutionary figures. Known as the "Father of Taiwanese Photography," he completely changed the face of the art form with his avant-garde concepts and experimental image language.

In 1965, the 22-year-old Chang Chao-tang and his teacher **Cheng Sang-hsi** (鄭桑溪) held the "Modern Photography Duo Exhibition." This exhibition was epoch-making in the history of Taiwanese photography; its themes and contents were vastly different from the mainstream salon style of the time, sparking intense controversy and widespread attention.

### Surrealist Image Experiments

Chang's early experimental works were deeply influenced by existentialist thought and surrealist aesthetics. His photos were filled with bewilderment, depression, and absurdity, exploring the meaning of existence through unique compositions and symbolic elements.

The most famous example is his "Headless Man" series from the 1960s. In these photos, the human head is deliberately obscured or cut off, creating a strong visual impact. Chang later explained: "That is a fragmented living body, placed in an environment much larger than him... What would he feel? What is he thinking?" This philosophical reflection on the human condition is the core quality of Chang's work.

## The Poet of the Land: Juan I-jong's Humanistic Documentary

### Recorder of Rural Life and Human Sentiment

While Chang Chao-tang opened a new realm of modern photography with his experimental language, **Juan I-jong** (阮義忠, born 1950) added a warm human touch with his deep humanistic concern and poetic documentary style.

Juan's most important work, _Man and Land_ (人與土地, 1987), is hailed as a classic in the history of Taiwanese photography. It captures the true face of Taiwanese society during its transition in the 1970s and 80s. In an era of rapid economic development and social change, Juan chose to aim his lens at traditional lifestyles that were about to disappear, recording the simplest and truest state of the Taiwanese people.

These photos depict rural pastoral scenery, the focused expressions of traditional craftsmen, and the innocent play of children. Juan used his keen observation and deep humanistic literacy to transform these ordinary moments into eternal works of art.

## Contemporary Vision: Diverse Explorations of the New Generation

### Shen Chao-liang: Poet of Landscape and Memory

Among contemporary Taiwanese photographers, **Shen Chao-liang** (沈昭良, born 1968) is one of the most internationally renowned. His works, from the early _STAGE_ (台灣綜藝團) to the recent _Drifting_ (漂流) series, show his deep observation of society and culture. The _STAGE_ series records the situation of traditional performing arts in modern society, reflecting the complexity of Taiwan's cultural transition through marginalized performance groups.

### Challenges of the Digital Age

Entering the 21st century, the popularity of digital photography technology has brought new opportunities and challenges to Taiwanese photography. New generation photographers are actively exploring various possibilities: some focus on the inheritance and innovation of traditional skills, some try cross-media artistic experiments, and some focus on local identity issues under the background of globalization.

## Conclusion: The Spirit of Taiwan in Light and Shadow

Reviewing the history of Taiwanese photography, we see not only technological progress and aesthetic evolution but also a microcosm of the changes in Taiwan's social culture. Taiwanese photographers have used their lenses to record the beauty and sorrow of this island and witnessed the struggle and growth of the Taiwanese people. Their works are not only artistic creations but also cultural heritage, an important part of the collective memory of the Taiwanese people.
