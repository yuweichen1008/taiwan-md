# Literature During Japanese Rule

In the spring of 1895, Taiwan's literary destiny began a completely new chapter with the signing of the Treaty of Shimonoseki. If Taiwanese literature during the Qing period was slowly nurtured within the womb of traditional Chinese culture, then the fifty years of Japanese rule saw Taiwanese literature painfully yet gloriously born as an independent literary life under the intense impact of Western modernity.

This was the most complex, contradictory, and dramatic era in Taiwanese literary history. The colonizer's language became a tool for the colonized to express resistance; new literary concepts brought by oppressors inspired national awakening among the oppressed; the most profound cultural identity often emerged from the most intense cultural conflicts. During these fifty years, Taiwanese literature experienced multiple transformations—from classical to modern, from classical Chinese to vernacular, from Chinese to Japanese—and ultimately found its unique voice within multiple cultural tensions.

## The Cession of 1895: Literary Historical Rupture

In 1895, with the echoes of gunfire from the First Sino-Japanese War still resonating, the signing of the Treaty of Shimonoseki pushed Taiwan onto another historical trajectory. For Taiwan's literati, this represented not only political upheaval but a fundamental turning point in literary destiny.

### Tragic Voices of Anti-Japanese Literature

"Spring sorrows hard to dispel, forced to view mountains, past events startle the heart, tears about to flow. Four million people cry as one, today marks one year since Taiwan's cession." These lines from Qiu Fengjia (丘逢甲) in his poem "Spring Sorrow" (春愁) capture the grief and helplessness of Taiwanese intellectuals facing Taiwan's cession.

During 1895, Taiwan saw numerous anti-Japanese literary works emerge. These works, primarily poetry, expressed resistance to Japanese rule and longing for the motherland. Lian Yatang's (連雅堂) _Preface to the General History of Taiwan_ (台灣通史序), though written later, had spiritual origins traceable to this period. The lament "Taiwan has no history" reflected intellectuals' cultural self-awareness in establishing history and literature for Taiwan.

However, more literati chose to "return inland" to the mainland. This massive cultural brain drain caused temporary discontinuity in Taiwan's literary development. Literati who remained in Taiwan either gradually fell silent under Japanese cultural policies or struggled painfully in conflicts between tradition and modernity, local and foreign influences.

## New vs. Old Literature Debate: Baptism by Modernity

### Zhang Wojun: Pioneer of the New Literature Movement

In 1924, an article titled "A Letter to Taiwan's Youth" was published in _Taiwan People's News_ (台灣民報), written by Zhang Wojun (張我軍), who had just returned from Beijing. This article fiercely criticized Taiwan's traditional poetry and prose as "decadent and unbearable," advocating learning from the new literature promoted by Hu Shi (胡適) and others, using vernacular Chinese for creative writing.

Zhang Wojun's views sparked intense debate. Traditional literati led by Lian Yatang firmly opposed, believing vernacular Chinese was "shallow and tasteless," damaging the depth of Chinese culture. The new literature faction led by Zhang Wojun argued that only vernacular Chinese could express modern people's thoughts and emotions, truly bringing literature to the masses.

This debate's significance transcended literature itself. It was essentially a fundamental discussion about Taiwan's cultural development direction: Should Taiwan preserve tradition or embrace modernity? Maintain elite culture or move toward popular culture? Adhere to "national language" (Chinese) or accept "national language" (Japanese)?

### _Taiwan People's News_: Cradle of New Literature

_Taiwan People's News_, founded in 1925, became an important base for the new literature movement. This newspaper not only published political news but, more importantly, provided a platform for Taiwan's new literature. Many important writers published their debut works in this newspaper.

The literary section of _Taiwan People's News_ displayed obvious characteristics of its era: publishing new poetry, novels, and essays using vernacular Chinese while retaining sections for traditional poetry, reflecting transitional features of old and new coexistence. More importantly, this newspaper began focusing on Taiwan's local social reality, laying the foundation for realist literature's emergence.

## Lai He: Father of Taiwan's New Literature

If one were to select the most important Taiwanese writer during Japanese rule, Lai He (賴和, 1894-1943) would undoubtedly be the top choice. This country doctor from Changhua laid the foundation for Taiwan's new literature through his literary works, earning the honor of being called "Father of Taiwan's New Literature."

### Literary Enlightenment and National Awakening

Lai He's literary creation was inseparable from his political awakening. In 1921, he participated in Taiwan Cultural Association activities and began focusing on Taiwan's social problems. In 1925, he published the essay "Untitled" (無題) and the new poem "Sacrifice Under Awakening—To Comrades in the Erlien Incident" (覺悟下的犧牲——寄二林事件的戰友), formally entering new literature creation.

Lai He's representative work "Fighting and Making Merry" (鬥鬧熱, 1926) is considered Taiwan's first true vernacular novel. Using a temple festival as background, this novel described the lifestyle of Taiwanese people while skillfully interspersing criticism of Japanese rule. The novel's language was vivid and lively, extensively using Taiwanese vocabulary, pioneering the localization of Taiwanese literature.

### Foundation of Realism

Lai He's literary creation consistently focused on social reality, particularly the living difficulties of common people. His novel "A Steelyard" (一桿「稱仔」) described vendor Qin Decan's unfair treatment due to weights and measures reform, profoundly exposing the absurdity and cruelty of colonial rule.

This realist writing tendency established an important tradition for Taiwanese literature. It was not art for art's sake but literature's practice of intervening in reality, reflecting people's sufferings, and enlightening public wisdom. This tradition later influenced numerous writers like Yang Kui (楊逵) and Lü Heruo (呂赫若), becoming an important characteristic of Taiwanese literature.

### Language Experimentation and Innovation

Lai He's linguistic experimentation held pioneering significance. His works extensively used Taiwanese vocabulary and expressions, sometimes directly using Chinese characters for Taiwanese phonetic transliteration. These attempts caused considerable controversy at the time but established the direction for Taiwanese literature's linguistic localization.

More importantly, through language experimentation, Lai He found appropriate ways to express the Taiwanese experience. His literary language maintained Chinese literature's literary quality while incorporating Taiwanese's vividness, creating a modern Chinese with local characteristics.

## Rise of Left-Wing Literature

### Yang Kui: Standard-Bearer of Proletarian Literature

Yang Kui (楊逵, 1906-1985) was an important representative of Taiwan's left-wing literature. He studied in Japan in his early years, encountered Marxist thought and proletarian literary theory, and returned to Taiwan dedicated to socialist literature creation and promotion.

Yang Kui's representative work "The Paperboy" (送報伸) was Taiwan's first novel with a worker as protagonist. This work described the tragic fate of a newspaper delivery boy, profoundly exposing capitalist society's injustice. The novel's language was plain and powerful, with vivid character portrayals embodying proletarian literature's aesthetic characteristics.

In 1935, Yang Kui founded _Taiwan New Literature_ magazine, Taiwan's first purely literary magazine. The magazine not only published local writers' works but also extensively translated foreign left-wing literary works, providing international perspectives for Taiwanese literature.

### Zhang Shenqie and _Taiwan Literature_

In 1934, _Taiwan Literature_ (台灣文藝) magazine was founded. Founder Zhang Shenqie (張深切) proposed an editorial policy of "not distinguishing ideologies, not discussing factions," broadly uniting Taiwan's literary forces. This magazine became one of the most influential literary publications in Taiwanese literary history.

_Taiwan Literature_'s characteristic was inclusiveness and diversity. It published both left-wing writers' works and modernist-style creations; both Chinese and Japanese writings; focusing on both Taiwan's locality and world literature. This open attitude laid the foundation for Taiwan literature's diverse development.

## Literary Struggles During the Imperialization Period

### Rise of Japanese-Language Literature

After the outbreak of the Sino-Japanese War in 1937, Japan promoted the "Imperialization Movement" (皇民化運動) in Taiwan, prohibiting public use of Chinese and mandating Japanese education. Against this background, Taiwan saw a group of local writers creating in Japanese.

These writers faced extremely complex cultural situations. On one hand, they had to use the colonizer's language for creation; on the other hand, they hoped to maintain concern for Taiwan's local culture in their works. This contradiction produced unique literary tensions while creating some excellent works.

### Lü Heruo: Artist Crossing Languages

Lü Heruo (呂赫若, 1914-1951) was the most important writer of this period. His novel "The Ox Cart" (牛車) used delicate brushstrokes to describe Taiwanese rural decline, showing farmers' helplessness and struggles under modernization's impact. Although written in Japanese, the work's spiritual core remained deeply rooted in Taiwan's soil.

Lü Heruo's creation embodied colonial writers' cultural strategy: superficially cooperating with colonial policies while actually expressing protection of local culture through literary metaphor and symbolism. This "indirect writing" approach became an important tradition in later Taiwanese literature.

### Zhang Wenhuan: Literature's Persistent Advocate

Zhang Wenhuan (張文環, 1909-1978) was another important Japanese-language literary writer. His novel "The Castrated Rooster" (閹雞) used a castrated rooster's story to metaphorically represent Taiwanese people's fate under colonial rule. This symbolic technique both avoided political censorship and expressed profound political allegory.

In 1941, Zhang Wenhuan founded _Taiwan Literature_ (台灣文學) magazine, the most important literary publication during the imperialization period. Although using Japanese, the magazine maintained Taiwan's local literary stance, providing precious publication space for Taiwanese writers.

## Aesthetic Qualities of Colonial Literature

### Literary Expression of Double Consciousness

Taiwanese literature during Japanese rule had obvious "double consciousness" characteristics. Writers had to face colonial rule's reality while maintaining identification with local culture; learn modern literary techniques while adhering to national literary positions. This complex cultural situation produced unique literary aesthetics.

This double consciousness manifested at many levels: language choice (Chinese or Japanese), subject matter selection (reality or tradition), stance management (resistance or cooperation), style determination (local or international). It was within these complex choices that Taiwanese literature gradually formed its own characteristics.

### Deepening of Realism

Taiwanese literature during Japanese rule was dominated by realism. This realism was not simple life description but carried strong social criticism and national consciousness. Through literary works, writers exposed colonial rule's injustice, reflected people's suffering, and expressed pursuit of social justice.

This realist literature's characteristic was closely combining personal destiny with national destiny. A farmer's bankruptcy often hinted at entire agricultural society's decline; an intellectual's struggles often reflected the entire nation's predicament. This combination of grand narrative and personal narrative provided rich expressive space for Taiwanese literature.

### Localized Literary Language

Despite experiencing multiple language transitions, Taiwanese literature during Japanese rule consistently sought literary language suitable for expressing the Taiwanese experience. Whether Lai He's Taiwanese-Chinese, Yang Kui's popularized Chinese, or Lü Heruo's "Taiwanese-style Japanese," all embodied writers' persistent exploration of literary language localization.

This exploration's significance lay not only in finding expressive tools but in establishing Taiwanese literature's linguistic characteristics. What should Taiwanese literature's language be like? How can literary quality be maintained while embodying local characteristics? Exploration of these questions provided valuable experience for post-war Taiwanese literature development.

## Historical Turning Point

On August 15, 1945, with the Japanese Emperor's surrender announcement, Taiwan's Japanese rule period officially ended. For Taiwanese literature, this was both the end of one era and the beginning of another.

The fifty years of Japanese rule left rich legacies for Taiwanese literature: establishment of modern literary concepts, awakening of localization consciousness, tolerance for multiculturalism, and foundation of realist tradition. These all became important bases for post-war Taiwanese literature development.

Simultaneously, this period also exposed some problems in Taiwanese literature development: linguistic identity confusion, cultural identity ambiguity, and political stance complexity. These problems persisted after the war and appeared in new forms under different historical conditions.

## Conclusion: Blooming in the Cracks

Taiwanese literature during Japanese rule was a flower blooming in the cracks. It was caught between tradition and modernity, between local and foreign, between resistance and cooperation. It was within these complex tensions that Taiwanese literature found its own voice and established its own character.

Lai He's physician's pen, Yang Kui's fighting voice, Lü Heruo's artistic beauty, and Zhang Wenhuan's persistent will—these different literary faces together constitute the rich picture of Taiwanese literature during Japanese rule. Through their creations, they proved that even under the most difficult historical conditions, literature could still uphold its ideals and express national voices.

Taiwanese literature from this period laid a solid foundation for later development. Its realist tradition, localization consciousness, spirit of resistance, and cultural tolerance all became important characteristics of Taiwanese literature, influencing it to this day. As Lai He wrote in his poetry: "Warriors should fight for righteousness"—this literary courage and idealism remain Taiwanese literature's most precious treasures today.
