# Taiwan’s Campus Folk Song Movement: Singing Our Own Songs

In December 1976 at Tamkang College (淡江文理學院), a young Taiwanese student took the stage with a Coca‑Cola bottle in hand and a guitar on his back. He smashed the bottle and demanded: **“Why are we singing foreign songs?”**

That student was **Lee Shuang‑tse (李雙澤)**, and the moment became known as the **“Tamkang Incident (淡江事件).”** It did more than break glass—it shattered a cultural spell. It became the symbolic spark of Taiwan’s **Campus Folk Song Movement (民歌運動)**, a wave of musical and cultural self‑discovery that reshaped Taiwanese popular music.

## Why “Sing Our Own Songs”? The 1970s Turning Point

In the early 1970s, Taiwan’s airwaves were dominated by English‑language folk and pop. Young people could sing Bob Dylan and Joan Baez, but rarely heard songs about their own place. The island’s cultural voice felt secondary—borrowed rather than authored.

That sense of dislocation intensified during a series of diplomatic shocks: Taiwan’s exit from the United Nations, the rise of the “Diaoyutai” protest movement, and the gradual end of Taiwan’s diplomatic isolation with the U.S. These events led a generation to ask: **Who are we? What is our own voice?**

“Sing our own songs (唱自己的歌)” became not just a slogan but a cultural awakening.

## The Three Pioneers

### Yang Hsien (楊弦): The Seed Planter

On June 6, 1975, Yang Hsien, a graduate student at National Taiwan University, held a concert at Zhongshan Hall where he presented the idea of **“modern folk songs.”** He set the poet Yu Guangzhong’s “Nostalgia Suite (鄉愁四韻)” to music, blending Chinese modern poetry with Western folk idioms. This concert is widely viewed as the formal birth of the movement.

### Hu De‑fu (胡德夫): The First Voice

An Indigenous singer from the Puyuma people, Hu De‑fu brought the sound of Taiwan’s mountains and oceans into urban cafés. His 1974 concert **“Beautiful Rice Ears (美麗的稻穗)”** featured early performances of Yang Hsien’s work, linking Indigenous oral traditions to modern folk composition.

### Lee Shuang‑tse (李雙澤): The Revolutionary

Lee’s 1976 outburst at Tamkang was the movement’s most dramatic moment. It reframed folk music as cultural resistance. His demand—_sing our own songs_—would become a rallying cry for campus folk, and later generations of Taiwanese musicians.

## The Commercial Engine: Golden Rhythm Awards & Folk Cafés

Grassroots passion needed infrastructure. In 1977, the **Golden Rhythm Awards (金韻獎)**—launched by Hsing Ko Records—provided a formal platform for new folk singers. In 1978, Hai Shan Records launched **“Folk‑Style” (民謠風)** competitions. These events became talent incubators for a generation of artists.

At the same time, **folk cafés** blossomed across Taiwan—venues like **Wooden Boat (木船)** and **Scarecrow (稻草人)** in Taipei. These spaces were not just performance stages; they were cultural salons where young people exchanged ideas about identity, language, and art.

## The Golden Era: A Constellation of Voices

The movement quickly matured into a golden age, marked by multiple stylistic streams.

### The Literary School

Artists like **Chyi Yu (齊豫)** emphasized poetry and introspection. Her rendition of **“Olive Tree (橄欖樹)”** captured the yearning for freedom and distant horizons.

### The Rural School

Songs like **“Country Road (鄉間的小路)”** by Ye Chia‑hsiu and **“Grandma’s Penghu Bay (外婆的澎湖灣)”** by Pan An‑pang carried rural imagery and nostalgia, giving urban audiences a gentle return to the countryside.

### The Urban School

Singers like **Tsai Chin (蔡琴)** and **Cheng Yi (鄭怡)** pushed folk music into a more polished, metropolitan sensibility—less pastoral, more cosmopolitan.

### Iconic Works

- **“The Descendants of the Dragon (龍的傳人)”** – a pan‑Chinese identity anthem
- **“Olive Tree (橄欖樹)”** – longing and freedom
- **“Just Like Your Tenderness (恰似你的溫柔)”** – urban romance
- **“Autumn Cicada (秋蟬)”** – youth and melancholy

## Decline and Transformation (Early 1980s)

By the early 1980s, the campus folk movement began to fade. Several forces converged:

- **Talent drift:** many key musicians left for graduate study or military service.
- **Changing cultural climate:** the late 1980s saw a shift toward localized political consciousness and more direct social critique.
- **Commercialization:** the success of folk music encouraged formulaic production, dulling its early experimentation.

Yet decline did not mean disappearance—it meant transformation.

## The Legacy: A DNA for Taiwanese Music

### Lo Ta‑yu: The Heir of Rebellion

Lo Ta‑yu inherited the movement’s spirit but sharpened its edge. Albums like _Zhihu Zheye (之乎者也)_ and _Home (家)_ combined social critique with pop sensibility, ushering in a new era of Taiwanese popular music.

### Seeds of Indie and Alternative

Contemporary singer‑songwriters—**Cheer Chen (陳綺貞)**, **Deserts Chang (張懸)**, **Crowd Lu (盧廣仲)**—carry the same DNA: personal narratives, local references, and a belief that music should express lived experience.

### “Sing Our Own Songs” as a Permanent Ethos

The movement’s greatest legacy is its ethos. Whether in rock, hip‑hop, or indie pop, the question remains: **How do we sing the story of this place in our own voice?** That question continues to define Taiwan’s creative culture.

## Revival and Remembrance

Milestone concerts like **“Folk 40”** and **“Folk 50”** revive this repertoire for new generations. These commemorations are not mere nostalgia—they are reminders of a moment when music dared to ask big questions about identity.

## Conclusion: Music as Cultural Self‑Definition

The Campus Folk Song Movement lasted barely a decade, but it reshaped Taiwan’s musical landscape. It proved that sound can be an act of self‑definition—and that a small island can write its own soundtrack.

The shattered Coca‑Cola bottle at Tamkang still echoes today. It asks every Taiwanese artist the same question: **What is our voice, and how will we sing it?**
