# Taiwan’s Soundscape

## 30-Second Overview

Close your eyes—can you hear Taiwan? At 6:30 p.m., a slow piano melody glides through alleyways as neighbors carry their trash out to the garbage truck. Deep at night, temple drums in **Beiguan (北管)** thunder while neon-lit **electric flower trucks (電子花車)** flash before the gods. In the mountains, the Bunun people sing **Pasibutbut**, a ritual polyphony so rich it sounds like eight voices coming from four.

Taiwan’s soundscape is not background noise. It is a living cultural code—an archive of memory, migration, and identity.

**Signature sounds:**

- 🚛 **Garbage truck music**: Taiwan’s daily civic ritual
- 🏯 **Temple festivals**: Beiguan and Nanguan ensembles, percussion troupes
- 🏔️ **Indigenous polyphony**: Bunun _Pasibutbut_
- 🛵 **Scooter chorus**: the world’s densest motorcycle culture
- 🌙 **Night market calls**: the theater of everyday commerce

---

## Why It Matters

Every city has a sonic fingerprint—New York’s subway screech, Tokyo’s train jingles. Taiwan is unique because its soundscape layers four centuries of history at once: Austronesian chants, Han migration rituals, Japanese-era school songs, and modern urban improvisations. Listening to Taiwan is a form of cultural archaeology.

And many of these sounds are disappearing—erased by urban development, language loss, or generational drift. To record them is not nostalgia; it is preservation.

## Everyday Urban Codes

### The Garbage Truck Melody: Taiwan’s Civic Ritual

Taiwan is one of the only places in the world where garbage trucks play classical music to signal collection. Two melodies dominate:

1. **“Für Elise” (致愛麗絲)** — Beethoven. Common in northern and central Taiwan.
2. **“A Maiden’s Prayer” (少女的祈禱)** — Tekla Bądarzewska, 1856. Common in southern Taiwan.

Where you live determines which melody is _your_ daily call. This musical geography is oddly precise—northerners find “A Maiden’s Prayer” unfamiliar; southerners find “Für Elise” strange.

Since the 1997 “no garbage on the ground” policy, this music has become part of Taiwan’s daily social choreography: neighbors meet, chat, and take out trash together. It is public policy disguised as ritual.

**Why classical music?** In the 1980s, officials chose tunes that were easy to recognize, non-annoying, and free of copyright. It worked so well it became a national memory trigger.

### The Scooter Sea: 14 Million Engines

Taiwan has over **14 million motorcycles**, among the highest densities on Earth. The surge at a green light—hundreds of engines lifting in unison—is the island’s urban heartbeat. As electric scooters grow, this mechanical roar may soon become a nostalgic sound.

### The Campaign Truck: Democracy at Full Volume

During elections, campaign trucks blast candidate slogans through loudspeakers. It is loud, repetitive, and sometimes absurd—but it is also a direct signal of Taiwan’s democratic transformation. Under martial law, even a poster could be punished; now, the street becomes a political stage.

## Sacred Sound: Temples and Rituals

### Beiguan (北管): The Temple’s Rock Music

Beiguan is loud, percussive, and emotionally intense. If rock music is “maximum volume for maximum feeling,” Beiguan is Taiwan’s oldest rock. Its vocal style uses **zhengyin guanhua (正音官話)**—a historical Mandarin register rather than modern Taiwanese Hokkien.

**Origins & evolution:** Introduced during the Qing dynasty, Beiguan’s musical roots are complex—closer to Zhejiang and Guangdong theater traditions than Fujian. In Taiwan, it developed two major schools (xipi and fulu), and rival troupes sometimes fought violently—proof that music taste can be serious business.

### Nanguan (南管): The Ancient Chamber Music

Nanguan (also called **Nanyin**, 南音) is the opposite of Beiguan: refined, slow, and meditative. It preserves musical structures dating back to the Tang and Song dynasties and was inscribed on UNESCO’s Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2009.

Typical instrumentation (“upper four” ensemble):

- Horizontal pipa
- Sansian
- Dongxiao (bamboo flute, Tang-era size)
- Erxian
- Clapper board

Nanguan teaches listening as an act of patience—an antidote to modern attention scarcity. In Taiwan, **Lukang (鹿港)** is its most important living hub.

### Electric Flower Trucks (電子花車): Sacred Meets Nightclub

These neon-lit trucks appear in funerals or temple festivals. Dancers perform atop stages while electronic music blends with Taiwanese folk songs and even Buddhist chants. It looks surreal—but it follows a local logic: **a grand send-off honors both gods and the dead**. This is Taiwanese folk culture at its most vivid and misunderstood.

International media like BBC and Vice have covered it as a uniquely Taiwanese phenomenon.

## Indigenous Sound: Memory of the Land

### Bunun _Pasibutbut_: “Eight-Part Harmony” That Isn’t

Often called “eight-part harmony,” _Pasibutbut_ is actually **four-part singing** that produces **overtone resonance** so rich it appears as eight voices. In 1952, Japanese ethnomusicologist Kurosawa Takatomo submitted a recording to UNESCO, astonishing researchers worldwide.

Sung in a circle of 6–12 men, it is performed during millet harvest rituals to honor **Dihanin**, the sky deity. The myth says the sound came from nature itself—tree hollows, waterfalls, forest wind.

### Other Indigenous Traditions

- **Amis Ilisin** (丰年祭) features call-and-response singing.
- **Paiwan and Rukai nose flutes (雙管鼻笛)** are rare instruments played by nobles.
- **Tao (達悟) clapping songs** on Orchid Island (蘭嶼, Lanyu) echo the rhythms of the sea.

These sounds are not “folk art” but **living ecosystems** of cultural memory.

## Nature’s Sound Signatures

- **Spring thunder** and the drumbeat of monsoon rain on tin roofs.
- **Cicadas**: over 60 species; summer volume can reach 90 decibels.
- **Endemic birds** like the Taiwan Hwamei—among the island’s most celebrated songs.

Taiwan’s soundscape is vertical as well as horizontal: different elevations create distinct sonic bands.

---

## Disappearing Sounds

- The **knife sharpener’s horn** echoing “Sharpen scissors!”
- **Hardware store loudspeaker ads** in Taiwanese Hokkien.
- **Radio drama era**: glove puppetry and opera broadcasts that shaped an older generation.
- **Taiwanese language tone loss**: a sonic erosion of an eight-tone language (seven in practice).

When a language fades, the soundscape itself changes.

## Contemporary Sound Experiments

### Sound Art and Installation

Taiwan has an active sound art scene. Spaces like **C-LAB (空總台灣當代文化實驗場)** and festivals such as **Lacking Sound Festival (失聲祭)** keep experimental practices alive.

### Field Recording as Cultural Archive

Increasingly, artists capture disappearing sounds—from mountain forests to industrial districts—turning them into cultural documents.

### Interactive Sound Media

New media artists use sensors and real-time processing to turn audience movement into sound. Listening becomes embodied experience.

## Surprising Facts

- **Garbage truck music is globally unique**—a daily classical melody ritual for waste collection.
- **“Eight-part harmony” is actually four-part singing** amplified by overtones.
- **Nanguan is a “living fossil”** of East Asian chamber music.
- **Taiwan’s cicadas reach 90 dB**, comparable to a vacuum cleaner.
- **Taiwanese Hokkien has one of the richest tone systems** in everyday speech.
