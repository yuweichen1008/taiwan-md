# Taiwan's Marine Ecology

At dawn in Chenggong Harbor, Taitung, longline boats return with billfish and tuna. A skipper points to a dark blue ribbon offshore: “That’s the Kuroshio—the place where fish never end.” The Kuroshio Current, a warm stream flowing north from the Philippines, wraps Taiwan’s east coast with heat and nutrients. Taiwan is an island with 1,566 km of coastline, but its maritime territory is roughly five times its land area—an ocean realm that spans tropical and temperate ecosystems. From Kenting’s coral reefs to Orchid Island’s flying fish culture, from the Northeast Coast’s seaweed forests to the offshore canyons where sperm whales dive, Taiwan’s seas are a biodiversity archive for the western Pacific.

> Taiwan’s surrounding seas cover about 180,000 km². They host over 3,000 fish species, more than 500 coral species, and 30+ cetacean species—one of the highest densities on Earth. The Kuroshio alone brings an estimated 3.5 million tons of fishery resources each year, sustaining coastal fishing and marine tourism.

## Why this matters

For Taiwan, the ocean is a lifeline. It feeds coastal communities, shapes climate, and sustains cultural memory. The Tao (達悟) people’s flying-fish season and the Han Chinese Mazu sea cult both reflect an island formed by waves. But climate change, pollution, and overdevelopment are putting this blue territory under strain. Coral bleaching, plastic pollution, and habitat fragmentation are now everyday realities. Taiwan’s attempts to balance development and conservation offer lessons for any island nation navigating a warming sea.

## The Kuroshio: Taiwan’s oceanic artery

The Kuroshio (黑潮, “Black Current”) is the North Pacific’s most powerful warm current:

- Originates east of the Philippines
- Flows along Taiwan’s eastern edge and onward to Japan
- Moves up to ~5 km per hour with a volume comparable to 200 Yangtze Rivers
- Runs 2–3°C warmer than surrounding waters

**Ecological effects**: The current fuels plankton blooms that feed krill and small fish, which in turn support tuna, marlin, and sharks. It also carries fish larvae and eggs, replenishing coastal stocks. In deeper water, its influence extends beyond 500 meters, shaping deep-sea coral fields and cephalopod breeding grounds.

## Coral reefs at the edge of the tropics

Taiwan is home to the northernmost tropical reefs in East Asia. Key reef zones include:

**Kenting National Park** (southern tip)

- Taiwan’s largest reef system
- 60–80% live coral coverage in prime zones
- Over 60 stony coral species and 1,000+ fish species

**Xiaoliuqiu** (coral island)

- Taiwan’s only coral limestone island
- Major green sea turtle (Chelonia mydas) habitat

**Green Island (綠島)**

- Volcanic reefs and the famous “Giant Mushroom Rock” coral formations
- A rare seawater hot spring ecosystem

**Orchid Island (蘭嶼)**

- High coral coverage (often 90%+)
- Traditional Tao fishing practices intertwined with conservation

**Northeast Coast**

- Temperate coral communities and sea-fan forests
- Rich intertidal biodiversity

Reef life includes clownfish and damselfish, parrotfish and wrasse, giant clams, sea cucumbers, and delicate soft corals. These reefs are beautiful—but fragile. Rising sea temperatures and coastal pollution are major threats.

## Cetaceans and the whale‑watching coast

Taiwan’s waters host 38 cetacean species—about one‑third of the world’s total. Common species include spinner dolphins, bottlenose dolphins, and pantropical spotted dolphins. Larger whales such as sperm whales and occasional blue whales frequent the deep trenches off Hualien.

Whale watching has grown into a major eco‑tourism industry since the 1990s, with Hualien as the primary hub (sighting rates often above 85%). This industry not only provides alternative livelihoods for fishers but also serves as a moving classroom for marine education.

**Conservation challenges** include:

- Underwater noise from ships and sonar
- Entanglement in fishing gear
- Plastic ingestion and habitat degradation
- Climate‑driven shifts in migration routes

## Flying fish season: Tao ocean culture

From March to June, Orchid Island enters Rayon—the flying fish season. For the Tao people, this is not just fishing; it is an ocean calendar with strict taboos, rituals, and ecological wisdom:

- **Calling Fish Season** marks the fish’s arrival
- **Harvest Season** allows limited capture
- **End‑Eating Season** relies on preserved flying fish
- **Rest Season** prohibits fishing to allow reproduction

Traditional **tatala** plank boats, torch fishing, and strict gendered rituals preserve an ancient relationship with the sea. Flying fish (seven species recorded around Orchid Island) are keystone species—feeding larger predators and sea birds. The cultural rules act as an indigenous conservation framework, long before “sustainability” became a modern term.

## Major threats: pollution, climate, development

**Plastic pollution**: Roughly 60% of marine litter comes from river inputs. Sea turtles ingest plastic bags; seabirds and fish accumulate microplastics; coral reefs are smothered by drifting debris.

**Water quality degradation**: Industrial effluent, agricultural runoff, and urban sewage contribute to eutrophication and heavy‑metal buildup in port sediments.

**Climate change**: Taiwan’s seas have warmed by ~1.6°C over the last 30 years. Coral bleaching is more frequent, fish distributions are shifting northward, and sea level rise threatens coastal ecosystems.

## Conservation tools and community participation

Taiwan has built a network of marine protected areas and national marine parks, such as Dongsha Atoll and the South Penghu Marine Park. Community‑based conservation programs also matter: sea‑turtle protection on Xiaoliuqiu, reef monitoring (Reef Check), and citizen‑science dolphin sighting networks.

**Sustainable fisheries measures** include total allowable catch (TAC), seasonal closures, gear restrictions, and habitat restoration (artificial reefs, hatchery releases). These policies are still evolving—but the direction is clear: the ocean can no longer be treated as an infinite reservoir.

## Offshore wind and ecological balance

Taiwan’s offshore wind expansion is part of its energy transition, with large arrays planned off Changhua and Yunlin. The ecological trade‑off is complex:

**Potential benefits**:

- Turbine foundations act as artificial reefs
- No‑trawl zones can reduce fishing pressure
- Lower carbon emissions help mitigate climate change

**Risks**:

- Construction noise and seabed disruption
- Migration corridor interference (including the critically endangered Indo‑Pacific humpback dolphin, known locally as the “white dolphin”)
- Changes to local fisheries

Taiwan is now testing mitigation strategies—noise control, corridor design, and fishery compensation—to ensure green energy does not mean blue loss.

## A blue future worth protecting

Taiwan’s oceans are a living archive: black currents, coral gardens, flying fish rituals, and whale migrations. They are also a frontline—where climate change, industrial development, and conservation collide. Protecting the sea is not a side project; it is an island’s survival strategy.

As Taiwan learns to speak with the ocean’s grammar—through science, policy, and local wisdom—it builds a model for how islands can live with water rather than against it. The goal is not to freeze the ocean in time, but to keep it alive: vibrant enough to feed, inspire, and sustain future generations.
