# Taiwan Marine Conservation: Battles Against Pollution and Coral Bleaching

## At a Glance

Surrounded by the ocean, Taiwan boasts incredibly rich marine ecosystems but faces severe pollution challenges. From plastic waste and industrial runoff to overfishing, the pressure on its blue territory is immense. However, a strong response is underway: the establishment of the Ocean Affairs Council, the designation of marine national parks like Dongsha Atoll, and grassroots movements in places like Xiaoliuqiu and Green Island are turning the tide for coral reefs and sea turtles.

**Keywords:** Marine Debris, Coral Bleaching, Marine Protected Areas (MPAs), Dongsha Atoll, Xiaoliuqiu, Sea Turtles

## Why It Matters

For an island nation, the ocean is not just a boundary but a lifeline. Taiwan's fisheries, tourism, and shipping industries depend on a healthy marine environment. Moreover, as climate change accelerates, the ocean acts as a critical buffer. Taiwan’s experience in balancing economic development with marine conservation—especially its community-led success stories—offers valuable lessons for global ocean governance.

## The Blue Territory: Taiwan's Marine Ecosystems

### Geographic Advantage

Located at the edge of the continental shelf in the Western Pacific, Taiwan sits at a convergence zone of major ocean currents: the warm Kuroshio Current from the south, the China Coastal Current from the north, and currents from the South China Sea. This mix brings abundant nutrients, creating a biodiversity hotspot.

- **Territorial Sea:** 12 nautical miles (approx. 22,000 sq km)
- **EEZ:** 200 nautical miles (approx. 160,000 sq km)
- **Key Ecosystems:**
  - **Coral Reefs:** Xiaoliuqiu, Green Island, Orchid Island, Dongsha Atoll.
  - **Seagrass Beds:** Dapeng Bay, Sicao Wetlands.
  - **Deep Sea Canyons:** Off the east coast, home to unique deep-water species.

### Rich Biodiversity

According to the Ocean Affairs Council, Taiwan's waters host:

- **Fish:** Over 3,000 species (1/10th of the world's marine fish species).
- **Corals:** About 300 species of reef-building corals.
- **Marine Mammals:** Over 30 species, including various whales and dolphins.

## Major Challenges

### 1. The Plastic Plague

**The Crisis:**
Surveys show Taiwan’s coastline has an average density of 1,855 pieces of trash per kilometer, with plastics making up over 80%.

**Sources:**

- **Land-based (70-80%):** Waste carried by rivers into the sea.
- **Sea-based (15-20%):** Discarded fishing gear ("ghost nets") and ship waste.
- **Transboundary (5-10%):** Debris drifting from neighboring countries.

**Hotspots:**
West coast mudflats (trapping debris) and northern coasts (seasonal drift) are particularly affected.

### 2. Coral Bleaching

**The Phenomenon:**
Coral bleaching occurs when stressed corals expel the symbiotic algae living in their tissues, turning white. If the stress persists, the coral dies.

**History of Bleaching in Taiwan:**

- **1998:** A global mass bleaching event wiped out vast reef areas.
- **2020:** The Dongsha Atoll suffered a catastrophic mass bleaching event due to record-high sea temperatures.
- **2023:** Severe bleaching reported again in Kenting and Green Island.

**Drivers:**
Rising sea temperatures due to climate change are the primary culprit, exacerbated by local pollution (sediment, sewage) and physical damage from tourism.

## Governance: The Ocean Affairs Council

In April 2018, Taiwan took a historic step by establishing the **Ocean Affairs Council (OAC)**, a cabinet-level agency dedicated to integrating marine policy, which was previously scattered across various ministries.

**Key Arms:**

- **Ocean Conservation Administration (OCA):** Focuses on pollution control, biodiversity, and law enforcement.
- **Coast Guard Administration:** Enforces laws and conducts rescue operations.
- **National Academy of Marine Research:** Provides scientific backing for policy.

### Marine Protected Areas (MPAs)

Taiwan has designated several types of protected areas:

1. **Dongsha Atoll National Park (2007):**
   - A massive 353,000-hectare park in the South China Sea.
   - Hosts a complete atoll ecosystem and serves as a shark nursery.
2. **South Penghu Marine National Park (2014):**
   - Known for its basalt columns and vibrant coral reefs.
   - A sanctuary for terns (seabirds) and reef fish.

## The Xiaoliuqiu Miracle: A Community-Led Success

Xiaoliuqiu (Little Liuqiu), a coral island off the southwest coast, is Taiwan's shining example of community conservation.

**The Transformation:**
Once depleted by overfishing, the island is now a sanctuary for Green Sea Turtles. In 2009, fewer than 30 turtles were spotted; by 2023, the number soared to over 500 residents. You can literally stand on the shore and see turtles surfacing for air.

**How They Did It:**

- **Ban on Gillnets:** Local fishermen agreed to ban gillnets within 3 nautical miles to protect reefs and turtles.
- **Eco-Tourism:** The island rebranded from fishing to "turtle tourism."
- **Plastic Reduction:** Implementation of the "琉行杯" (Liuqiu Cup) rental system to reduce single-use plastics.
- **Local Patrols:** Community volunteers patrol beaches to protect nesting sites.

## Future Outlook

### 2050 Net-Zero Ocean

Taiwan aims to align with global standards, targeting zero marine plastic waste and promoting a circular economy. The "Marine Conservation Act" (currently being pushed) aims to provide a stronger legal framework for designating MPAs and regulating marine activities.

### Technology & Innovation

- **Satellite Monitoring:** Used to detect illegal oil dumping and monitor coral thermal stress.
- **Citizen Science:** Apps like "Marine Debris Rapid Survey" allow public participation in tracking pollution.

## Conclusion

Taiwan's battle for its blue territory is a mix of high-level policy and grassroots grit. The recovery of sea turtles in Xiaoliuqiu proves that marine ecosystems can bounce back when given a chance. As an island nation, Taiwan is learning that its future is not just on the land, but deeply tied to the health of the surrounding sea.
