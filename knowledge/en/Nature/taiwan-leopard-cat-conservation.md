# Taiwan Leopard Cat Conservation

## 30‑Second Overview

Taiwan once had clouded leopards. In 2013, they were officially declared extinct in the wild.

Now the island’s only remaining native wild cat is the **leopard cat** (石虎, _Prionailurus bengalensis_). Fewer than 500 are thought to remain—and the number is still shrinking.

The leopard cat does **not** live deep in remote mountains. It lives in low‑elevation foothills of Miaoli, Nantou, and Taichung—precisely the landscapes most exposed to farming, roads, and factory development. It dies crossing highways, loses hunting grounds to construction, gets poisoned for raiding chicken coops, and can be killed by free‑roaming dogs.

This is the sharpest frontline of Taiwan’s conservation debate: development vs. coexistence.

**Key facts:**

- Estimated population: **< 500**
- Main habitat: low‑elevation foothills in Miaoli, Nantou, Taichung
- Documented roadkills (2017–2023): **130+**
- Listed as a **Critically Endangered** protected species in Taiwan
- Last remaining native wild cat on the island

## Why it matters

The leopard cat is more than a charismatic animal. It is a **sentinel species** for Taiwan’s low‑mountain ecosystems—an indicator that frogs, rodents, birds, and insects are still part of an intact food web. When the leopard cat disappears, the entire ecological chain collapses with it.

But there is a deeper question: its habitat is not wilderness. It is people’s backyard. Protecting the leopard cat asks whether Taiwanese society can leave space for other species **right where humans live**. Taiwan already lost the clouded leopard. The leopard cat is the last warning—and the last chance.

## The “ghost cat” you rarely see

Despite the name, the leopard cat is about the size of a house cat (3–6 kg) and not closely related to leopards. It is distinguished by two white facial stripes running up the forehead, and “false eyes” (white spots) on the back of its ears. It is nocturnal, solitary, and territorial—males may range over 5–10 km². That’s why locals call it a “ghost cat”: it lives nearby, but most people never see one in their lifetime.

Its preferred habitat is **low‑elevation mosaic landscapes**: orchards, bamboo groves, small farms, and secondary forests under 800 meters. These are also Taiwan’s most heavily developed zones—densely networked by roads and industrial parks.

## Four main causes of death

### 1) Roadkill — the silent slaughter

Leopard cats cross roads at night to hunt, mate, and patrol territory. Rural highways often lack wildlife crossings or speed‑reduction design. When headlights freeze their eyes, the result is fatal.

Between 2017 and 2023, more than 130 leopard cat roadkills were recorded. The real number is likely higher, since many deaths go unreported.

### 2) Habitat loss — home erased

Miaoli is the leopard cat’s core habitat, yet also one of Taiwan’s most development‑conflicted counties. In 2019, the county government proposed a **Leopard Cat Conservation Local Ordinance**—and it was rejected twice by the county council on the grounds of “hindering development.”

This contradiction is Taiwan’s conservation dilemma: the leopard cat is used for tourism branding and mascots, but when protection threatens land development, the cat is pushed aside.

### 3) Dog attacks — the overlooked threat

Camera traps increasingly record free‑roaming dog packs inside leopard cat habitat. These dogs chase and kill leopard cats. Conservationists and animal‑welfare advocates face a difficult ethical conflict: protecting stray dogs can mean losing native wildlife.

The debate reveals a hard truth: “animal love” is not a simple position. It requires choices and trade‑offs.

### 4) Poison and traps

Leopard cats prey on chickens. Some farmers respond with poison. Despite a 2020 ban on steel traps, illegal traps still exist, causing severe injury and death.

## A ray of hope: people are protecting

### Ecological compensation

Taiwan’s Ministry of Agriculture launched an **Ecological Compensation Program** in 2021. Farmers who adopt wildlife‑friendly practices—no poison, no traps—receive subsidies (up to NT$20,000 per hectare). If camera traps record a leopard cat on their land, they receive additional payments.

This is not charity; it is a strategic alignment. The program turns farmers into conservation partners rather than adversaries.

### Wildlife crossings

Some Miaoli and Nantou roads now include underpasses and culverts designed for wildlife. Camera traps show leopard cats using them. But the number of safe crossings is still far below what the road network requires.

### “Star” leopard cats

Conservation needs narratives. Two well‑known leopard cats have helped:

- **Jibao** (集寶), rescued and cared for at the Taichung animal shelter, became a public ambassador.
- **A‑Hu** (阿虎) at the Endemic Species Research Institute is part of a captive‑breeding program.

These cats are visible because they can no longer survive in the wild—a reminder of how fragile the population has become.

## A local law that exposed the system

The 2019 Miaoli ordinance became a political mirror. The proposed law was modest: it required ecological assessments for developments within leopard cat habitat. Yet it was rejected twice. After public pressure, a third attempt passed in 2020, making it Taiwan’s first local leopard cat protection ordinance.

The controversy exposed a gap between **symbolic conservation** and **real protection**—a gap that still defines Taiwan’s struggle with biodiversity.

## Surprising facts

- 🐆 The leopard cat’s scientific name is _Prionailurus bengalensis_—not related to leopards.
- 👀 Night vision is about **6×** stronger than humans, yet bright headlights immobilize them.
- 🏠 A male’s territory can span **5–10 km²**—roughly the size of Taipei’s Da’an District.
- ☠️ The clouded leopard (_Neofelis nebulosa_) was declared extinct in Taiwan in 2013.
- 📸 Camera‑trap networks cover less than half of current leopard cat habitat.

## The last five hundred

When Taiwan lost the clouded leopard, no one got to say goodbye.

The leopard cat is still here—less than five hundred. It walks at orchard edges in Miaoli, hunts in bamboo groves in Nantou, and waits for a safe gap in traffic on roads you might drive every day.

Conservationists often say: “If we fail in this generation, the next one will only see leopard cats in museum drawers.” It is not rhetoric; it is arithmetic. With ongoing roadkill, shrinking habitat, and genetic bottlenecks, the margin is vanishing fast.

Taiwanese culture has a saying: “If you pray, you’ll be protected.” But leopard cats don’t pray. They cross roads in the dark, hoping for a passing moment of mercy.

Whether Taiwan can keep this species alive is a test of what kind of society it wants to be: one that celebrates wildlife in slogans, or one that leaves real space for life to continue.
