# Taiwan’s Coffee Industry

Taiwan’s coffee story begins under Japanese rule, when coffee trees were first planted as an agricultural experiment. More than a century later, Taiwan is a specialty‑coffee force. In 2016, barista **Wu Ze‑Lin (吳則霖)** won the World Barista Championship—an historic first for Asia—signaling that Taiwan had shifted from a coffee‑consuming island to a coffee‑producing and coffee‑innovating nation.

## Japanese‑Era Beginnings

In 1901, Japanese agronomist **Tashiro Yasumasa** introduced coffee to **Gukeng (古坑)** in Yunlin. The island’s subtropical climate and mid‑elevation mountains seemed ideal. Early plantings focused on 600–1,200 meters in Gukeng, Guoxing (Nantou), and Taimali (Taitung), using Arabica varieties and new cultivation methods.

After World War II, coffee cultivation declined due to market shifts and technical gaps. Many farms switched to other crops, and Taiwan’s coffee faded for decades—until a revival began in the 1980s.

## Gukeng: A Revival Built on Local Taste

In the 1980s, Gukeng reclaimed coffee as a regional identity. Its mild acidity and gentle fruit notes aligned with local preferences. Lower elevation yields beans with lighter density and a softer, sweeter cup profile—floral, clean, and approachable.

The 1999 Chi‑Chi earthquake devastated the region but also triggered reconstruction programs that gave coffee new momentum. By the 2000s, Gukeng had become a coffee tourism destination, with cafés and annual festivals celebrating local beans.

## Alishan: Specialty Coffee at High Altitude

Alishan’s coffee follows a different path: specialty and terroir. Grown at 1,000–1,600 meters, beans develop slowly in large day‑night temperature swings. The result is dense beans with layered flavor—floral aromatics, bright acidity, and mineral nuance.

Producers increasingly experiment with processing methods—washed, honey, and natural—creating a palette of profiles. In 2020, **Zhouzhu Garden Coffee (鄒築園)** scored 90+ in the SCAA evaluation, proving Taiwan’s quality on an international stage.

## Simple Kaffa and the World Champion Moment

In 2016, **Simple Kaffa** in Taipei captured the global spotlight. Its barista, Wu Ze‑Lin, won the World Barista Championship in Dublin. The competition coffee was Panama Geisha, but the victory was ultimately about craft: precision, storytelling, and an obsessive commitment to flavor.

Simple Kaffa, founded in 2011, had already shaped a new coffee culture—one that prizes quality, training, and innovation over volume. Wu’s win catalyzed the entire industry, inspiring new competitions, new cafés, and a deeper public appreciation for specialty coffee.

## Taiwan’s Third‑Wave Character

Third‑wave coffee emphasizes origin, process, and flavor clarity. Taiwan’s version is distinctly local:

- **Local terroir pride**: cafés and roasters actively promote Taiwan‑grown beans alongside global imports.
- **Technical experimentation**: Taiwanese baristas innovate with brewing techniques and tools, adding global influence from a local perspective.
- **Education culture**: classes, cuppings, and public tastings cultivate consumer literacy, turning coffee into a shared language.

## Consumption Growth and Everyday Coffee

According to the International Coffee Organization (ICO), annual per‑capita consumption rose from 28 cups (2000) to 122 cups (2020), a 300% jump. Growth is driven by:

- **Chain cafés**: Starbucks, 85°C, Louisa, and cama make coffee ubiquitous.
- **Office culture**: coffee is a daily rhythm for Taiwan’s workforce.
- **Convenience stores**: 7‑Eleven’s City Café and FamilyMart’s Let’s Café make freshly brewed coffee accessible and affordable.

Taiwan’s hand‑shaken drink culture also nudged coffee forward; as people grew used to “a drink in hand,” coffee became a natural option.

## An Integrated Industry Chain

Taiwan now supports a full coffee ecosystem—from cultivation and import logistics to roasting, training, and equipment manufacturing.

- **Roasting excellence**: Taiwanese roasters win international awards, and brands like **Xingbo (興波)** and **OKLAO (歐客佬)** export globally.
- **Equipment design**: Taiwan produces respected brewing tools and machines, contributing to global supply chains.
- **Education and certification**: universities, vocational schools, and coffee associations build professional talent.

The industry has matured beyond cafés into a knowledge economy—one where Taiwan exports not only beans, but also expertise.

## A Century of Evolution

From Japanese‑era test plots to world championships, Taiwan coffee is a story of persistence and reinvention. Its future lies in deepening terroir identity, strengthening sustainable farming, and continuing to translate global techniques into a distinctly Taiwanese expression.

Taiwan’s coffee now tastes like the island itself: gentle yet complex, modest yet ambitious, and always evolving.
